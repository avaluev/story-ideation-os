# Anomaly 5 — Adderall/ADHD Shortage + DEA + Done Health Investigation

**Tier-1 PRIMARY sources:**
- FDA Drug Shortage List — Adderall (mixed amphetamine salts) listed October 2022 – present (as of mid-2026, 4+ year ongoing shortage)
- DEA Production Quota Final Orders 2022-2025 — Federal Register documentation of stimulant manufacturing limits
- DOJ press release (November 2023) — federal grand jury indictment of Done Health/Done Global executives (Ruthia He, David Brody) for distributing Adderall via telehealth without legitimate medical purpose, in violation of Controlled Substances Act
- Senate Finance Committee hearing transcripts (May 2023, March 2024) — Senator Wyden examining DEA quota decisions and pharma-supply consolidation
- US Attorney Northern District of California unsealed indictment (June 2024) — Done Health follow-up charges

**300-word summary:**

By October 2022, the FDA officially listed Adderall (mixed amphetamine salts) on its drug-shortage list. By mid-2024, the shortage had expanded to Vyvanse (lisdexamfetamine), Ritalin (methylphenidate), and Concerta — affecting an estimated 30 million US adults and 6 million children with ADHD prescriptions. Pharmacies began rationing partial fills (10 of 30 pills); patients reported driving 100+ miles to find medication.

Two parallel forces created the shortage: (1) DEA production quotas — the agency limits manufacture of Schedule II stimulants; the quota for 2023 was reduced citing "diversion concerns," but pharma-industry data showed the legal-prescription market had grown 25% during COVID-era telehealth expansion; (2) the rapid growth of telehealth ADHD prescribers — Done Health (founded 2019), Cerebral, Klarity Health — who, per FBI investigation, had been writing prescriptions in 30-minute video visits with no follow-up care, then dispensing through partner pharmacies.

In November 2023, DOJ unsealed a federal indictment against Done Health's CEO (Ruthia He) and Clinical President (David Brody) for distributing Adderall to people who did not medically need it, generating "substantial revenue from issuing such prescriptions over a brief period." This was the first such indictment against telehealth executives. Done Health's largest shareholder was Lerer Hippeau (NYC venture capital). The patient base — including thousands legitimately diagnosed during COVID — found their medication suddenly unobtainable post-indictment, as Done's prescribing license was suspended.
