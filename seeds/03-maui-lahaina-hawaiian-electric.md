# Anomaly 3 — Maui Lahaina Wildfires + Hawaiian Electric

**Tier-1 PRIMARY sources:**
- Hawaii Attorney General Phase Two Report (April 2024) — root cause analysis of the August 8, 2023 fire ignition
- Maui County v. Hawaiian Electric et al. — court filings, plaintiff steering committee discovery
- $4.04 billion global settlement (August 2024) — Hawaiian Electric Industries + Maui County + Hawaiian Telcom + property owners
- NIST Fire Investigation Report (2024) — power-line ignition forensics; downed-line + dry-grass fuel-load analysis

**300-word summary:**

On August 8, 2023, sustained 60-80mph trade winds (Hurricane Dora's outflow) snapped Hawaiian Electric's wooden poles in Lahaina, Maui. The 6:30am downed-line ignition was reported and "contained" by the Maui County Fire Department by morning; firefighters left around noon. The fire reignited from smoldering grass at 2:55pm and ran 1.7 miles through Lahaina town in approximately 7 hours. 102 people died — the deadliest US wildfire in over a century.

Hawaiian Electric had identified its grid as a high-fire-risk corridor years earlier and had created a "Public Safety Power Shutoff" (PSPS) program — but had NOT triggered it on August 8 despite National Weather Service red-flag warnings. Internal communications (subpoenaed in subsequent litigation) showed engineers had specifically warned that day. Maui Fire Department's "contained" call relied on visual inspection only; no thermal imaging was used to verify.

The Hawaii AG report concluded the fire was preventable. The $4.04B global settlement (August 2024) involved Hawaiian Electric, Maui County, Hawaiian Telcom, Kamehameha Schools, and West Maui Land Co. Hawaiian Electric stock dropped 75%; the company faced credit rating collapse.

Lahaina was the historic capital of the Hawaiian Kingdom — many homes and the Waiola Church (1828) were destroyed. The "rebuild Lahaina" debate became a proxy for native-Hawaiian land sovereignty against developer encroachment.
