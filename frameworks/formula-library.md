> `FORMULA.md` at the top of `ideas/{date}/{slug}/`, never reuse a formula in

## §A — Polti's 36 Dramatic Situations

Georges Polti's 1895 catalog of every dramatic conflict ever written. Each entry: structural template, 1-2 cinema examples, modal emotional arc, antagonist relationship.

### A.01 — Supplication
- Template: petitioner approaches power-holder seeking aid; power-holder weighs cost vs duty
- Examples: *Casablanca* (1942), *Children of Men* (2006)
- Modal arc: Tragedy or Cinderella
- Antagonist: power-holder (shared goal: keep the city; opposite method)
- Anti-pattern: petitioner-as-passive-witness (PM-D08 violation)

### A.02 — Deliverance
- Template: a hero rescues an unfortunate from a threatening agent
- Examples: *Schindler's List* (1993), *Argo* (2012)
- Modal arc: Cinderella or Man-in-Hole

### A.03 — Crime Pursued by Vengeance
- Template: an avenger hunts the criminal who wronged a kinsman
- Examples: *Kill Bill* (2003), *The Northman* (2022), *Memento* (2000)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.04 — Vengeance Taken for Kin upon Kin
- Template: kin-on-kin vengeance with hidden recognition
- Examples: *The Godfather* (1972), *Hamlet*-derived films
- Modal arc: Oedipus

### A.05 — Pursuit
- Template: a fugitive flees a pursuer; both want the same outcome (justice/freedom) by opposite means
- Examples: *The Fugitive* (1993), *Catch Me If You Can* (2002)
- Modal arc: Man-in-Hole

### A.06 — Disaster
- Template: a vanquished power suffers consequences; survivors must rebuild
- Examples: *The Pianist* (2002), *Children of Men* (2006)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.07 — Falling Prey to Cruelty/Misfortune
- Template: an innocent is destroyed by larger forces (natural, social, economic)
- Examples: *Precious* (2009), *The Grapes of Wrath* (1940)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.08 — Revolt
- Template: a tyrant is opposed; conspirators carry the revolt
- Examples: *V for Vendetta* (2005), *Spartacus* (1960)
- Modal arc: Cinderella or Icarus

### A.09 — Daring Enterprise
- Template: a leader undertakes an apparently impossible mission
- Examples: *Ocean's Eleven* (2001), *The Italian Job* (2003)
- Modal arc: Cinderella

### A.10 — Abduction
- Template: an abductor takes the loved one; rescuer pursues
- Examples: *Taken* (2008), *Prisoners* (2013)
- Modal arc: Man-in-Hole

### A.11 — The Enigma
- Template: an inquirer faces a problem requiring solution
- Examples: *Memento* (2000), *Mulholland Drive* (2001), *Citizen Kane* (1941)
- Modal arc: Oedipus or Icarus

### A.12 — Obtaining
- Template: a solicitor argues with an adversary holding the prize
- Examples: *Erin Brockovich* (2000), *The Insider* (1999)
- Modal arc: Cinderella

### A.13 — Enmity of Kinsmen
- Template: a malevolent kinsman opposes a hated kinsman
- Examples: *East of Eden* (1955), *The Lion King* (1994), *There Will Be Blood* (2007)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.14 — Rivalry of Kinsmen
- Template: two kinsmen compete for the same prize
- Examples: *Cain & Abel*-derived; *The Social Network* (2010)
- Modal arc: Icarus

### A.15 — Murderous Adultery
- Template: betrayal in love leads to murder
- Examples: *Double Indemnity* (1944), *Body Heat* (1981)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.16 — Madness
- Template: a kinsman of the mad person witnesses the descent
- Examples: *A Beautiful Mind* (2001), *Black Swan* (2010)
- Modal arc: Icarus or Tragedy

### A.17 — Fatal Imprudence
- Template: an imprudent act creates an irreversible loss
- Examples: *Manchester by the Sea* (2016), *Hereditary* (2018)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.18 — Involuntary Crimes of Love
- Template: a discoverer learns a beloved has done a forbidden thing
- Examples: *Atonement* (2007), *Closer* (2004)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.19 — Slaying of a Kinsman Unrecognized
- Template: a slayer recognizes the victim too late
- Examples: *Oedipus Rex*-derived; *Bird Box* (2018) variants
- Modal arc: Oedipus

### A.20 — Self-Sacrifice for an Ideal
- Template: a hero gives life/career for a cause
- Examples: *Schindler's List* (1993), *Selma* (2014), *The Insider* (1999)
- Modal arc: Cinderella

### A.21 — Self-Sacrifice for Kindred
- Template: a hero sacrifices for family or kin
- Examples: *Sophie's Choice* (1982), *Manchester by the Sea* (2016)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.22 — All Sacrificed for a Passion
- Template: a hero destroys life and reputation for love or obsession
- Examples: *Phantom Thread* (2017), *Whiplash* (2014)
- Modal arc: Icarus

### A.23 — Necessity of Sacrificing Loved Ones
- Template: a hero must choose to lose what they love most
- Examples: *Sophie's Choice* (1982), *The Last of Us* (HBO 2023, premiere)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.24 — Rivalry of Superior vs Inferior
- Template: an underdog challenges the established
- Examples: *Rocky* (1976), *The Devil Wears Prada* (2006), *Whiplash* (2014)
- Modal arc: Cinderella

### A.25 — Adultery
- Template: two lovers betray a deceived spouse
- Examples: *Brokeback Mountain* (2005), *Eyes Wide Shut* (1999)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.26 — Crimes of Love
- Template: a lover commits a forbidden act for love
- Examples: *Romeo and Juliet* (any film), *The Reader* (2008)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.27 — Discovery of the Dishonor of a Loved One
- Template: a discoverer learns a beloved has fallen in their estimation
- Examples: *Manchester by the Sea* (2016), *The Father* (2020)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.28 — Obstacles to Love
- Template: two lovers cannot unite due to circumstance
- Examples: *Casablanca* (1942), *Brokeback Mountain* (2005), *Past Lives* (2023)
- Modal arc: Tragedy or Cinderella

### A.29 — An Enemy Loved
- Template: a lover learns the beloved is the enemy
- Examples: *Mr. & Mrs. Smith* (2005), *The Americans* (FX, premise)
- Modal arc: Icarus

### A.30 — Ambition
- Template: an ambitious one is opposed by a counter-ambition
- Examples: *There Will Be Blood* (2007), *The Social Network* (2010), *Whiplash* (2014)
- Modal arc: Icarus

### A.31 — Conflict with God
- Template: a mortal struggles with the divine or natural order
- Examples: *Silence* (2016), *The Tree of Life* (2011)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.32 — Mistaken Jealousy
- Template: a jealous one wrongs an innocent
- Examples: *Othello*-derived; *Gone Girl* (2014, inverted)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.33 — Erroneous Judgment
- Template: a mistaken accuser destroys a real innocent
- Examples: *12 Angry Men* (1957), *The Hunt* (2012)
- Modal arc: Tragedy or Cinderella

### A.34 — Remorse
- Template: a culprit must reckon with what they did
- Examples: *Atonement* (2007), *Manchester by the Sea* (2016), *The Father* (2020)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### A.35 — Recovery of a Lost One
- Template: a seeker reunites with a lost loved one
- Examples: *Lion* (2016), *Arrival* (2016)
- Modal arc: Cinderella

### A.36 — Loss of Loved Ones
- Template: a kinsman loses a loved one and must inherit something from them
- Examples: *The Lion King* (1994), *Up* (2009), *Manchester by the Sea* (2016)
- Modal arc: Tragedy → Cinderella
- **Boeing "The Fastener" formula** — Hannah inherits Mitch's testimony at the wake

## §B — Reagan/Mitchell/Danforth/Dodds 6 Emotional Arcs (2016)

The 6 empirical arc shapes derived from sentiment analysis of 1,737 Project Gutenberg texts. Del Vecchio (2021) extended this to 6,174 films via subtitle analysis. **Man-in-Hole is the strongest commercial signal across genre and budget.**

### B.01 — Man-in-Hole (fall → rise)
- Empirical signal: STRONGEST commercial predictor (Del Vecchio 2021); most-talked-about, most-virally-shared
- Shape: ↘↗
- Examples: *Forrest Gump*, *The Pursuit of Happyness*, *Slumdog Millionaire*, *Hidden Figures*, *Erin Brockovich*, *Cast Away*
- When to use: default if seed evidence does not demand otherwise (per L011)
- When NOT to use: tragedy seeds (Boeing whistleblower → B.02 Tragedy)

### B.02 — Tragedy / Riches-to-Rags (steady fall)
- Shape: ↘
- Examples: *Romeo and Juliet*, *There Will Be Blood*, *Citizen Kane*, *Boogie Nights*
- When to use: seeds with documented downward trajectory (Boeing whistleblower; OpenAI board coup loss)
- Empirical signal: high critical-canon rate, lower box office mean

### B.03 — Cinderella (rise-fall-rise)
- Shape: ↗↘↗
- Examples: *Rocky*, *The Pursuit of Happyness*, *Hidden Figures*, *Slumdog Millionaire*
- Empirical signal: 2nd-strongest commercial predictor; highest Project Gutenberg downloads in compound form

### B.04 — Icarus (rise → fall)
- Shape: ↗↘
- Examples: *Citizen Kane*, *Whiplash*, *The Wolf of Wall Street*, *Goodfellas*, *The Social Network*
- Empirical signal: strong critical canon; mixed commercial

### B.05 — Rags-to-Riches (steady rise)
- Shape: ↗
- Examples: *Slumdog Millionaire* (partial), *The Theory of Everything*, *La La Land* (subverted)
- Empirical signal: lower than Man-in-Hole; audiences want the fall before the rise

### B.06 — Oedipus (fall-rise-fall)
- Shape: ↘↗↘
- Examples: *Oedipus Rex*-derived, *Vertigo*, *Memento*, *The Sixth Sense*
- Empirical signal: high in literature; rare in commercial cinema due to refusal of resolution

### B.07 — Compound: Cinderella + Man-in-Hole (Reagan 2016)
- Shape: ↗↘↗ + ↘↗ (sequential)
- Empirical signal: highest Project Gutenberg downloads — compound arcs WIN at virality
- Examples: *Slumdog Millionaire*, *Forrest Gump*

### B.08 — Compound: Two sequential Man-in-Hole
- Shape: ↘↗↘↗
- Empirical signal: 2nd-highest viral performance (Reagan 2016)
- Examples: *The Shawshank Redemption*, *Cast Away*

## §C — Pearson's 12 Archetypes

Carol Pearson's 12 universal archetypes — every protagonist maps to ONE primary archetype with secondary characteristics from at most 1-2 others.

### C.01 — The Innocent
- Core: trust, optimism, simplicity
- Examples: Forrest Gump, Amélie, Mr. Bean
- Modal arc: Cinderella

### C.02 — The Orphan / Everyman
- Core: belonging, fairness, realism
- Examples: Frodo, Bilbo, Andy Dufresne (*Shawshank*)
- Modal arc: Man-in-Hole

### C.03 — The Hero / Warrior
- Core: courage, mastery, prove worth
- Examples: Luke Skywalker, Katniss Everdeen, Wonder Woman
- Modal arc: Cinderella or Hero's Journey

### C.04 — The Caregiver
- Core: protection of others
- Examples: Erin Brockovich, Mary Poppins
- Modal arc: Man-in-Hole

### C.05 — The Seeker / Explorer
- Core: discovery, freedom, autonomy
- Examples: Indiana Jones, Captain Kirk, Neo (early *Matrix*)
- Modal arc: Cinderella

### C.06 — The Lover
- Core: passion, intimacy, devotion
- Examples: Romeo, Jack (*Titanic*)
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### C.07 — The Destroyer / Outlaw
- Core: revolution, breaking the false
- Examples: Tyler Durden, Walter White (*Breaking Bad*), Joker (Phoenix 2019)
- Modal arc: Icarus

### C.08 — The Creator / Artist
- Core: imagination, vision, originality
- Examples: Charlie Kaufman avatars, *Whiplash* Andrew, *Black Swan* Nina
- Modal arc: Icarus

### C.09 — The Ruler
- Core: control, order, sovereignty
- Examples: Don Corleone, Daniel Plainview
- Modal arc: Tragedy

### C.10 — The Magician / Visionary
- Core: transformation, power, knowledge
- Examples: Gandalf, Yoda, Doc Brown
- Modal arc: mentor (rarely protagonist)

### C.11 — The Sage
- Core: truth, wisdom, understanding
- Examples: Atticus Finch, Dr. Strange (later)
- Modal arc: variable

### C.12 — The Fool / Jester
- Core: joy, lightness, lived-in-the-moment
- Examples: *Wedding Crashers*, *The Big Lebowski* Dude
- Modal arc: Man-in-Hole

## §D — McKee Controlling-Idea Shapes

Robert McKee identifies 4 master patterns for the controlling idea (the moral premise of the story).

### D.01 — Idealistic ("X triumphs because of Y virtue")
- Examples: *Hidden Figures* (excellence triumphs over racism), *Erin Brockovich* (truth triumphs over corporate concealment)
- Cinema use: ~40% of commercial cinema

### D.02 — Pessimistic ("X fails because of Y vice")
- Examples: *There Will Be Blood* (greed corrupts absolutely), *Chinatown* (truth cannot defeat power)
- Cinema use: ~25% (overrepresented in critical canon)

### D.03 — Ironic (Y is both virtue AND vice)
- Examples: *Whiplash* (mastery requires inhumanity), *The Social Network* (genius requires betrayal)
- Cinema use: ~20% (highest auteur-cinema rate)

### D.04 — Tragic Irony (Y triumphs but at impossible cost)
- Examples: *The Godfather* (family loyalty completed at cost of soul), *Schindler's List* (saving life at cost of conscience)
- Cinema use: ~15%

## §E — Truby's 7-Step Morality Chain

John Truby's structural skeleton — every protagonist passes through these 7 steps:

1. **Moral weakness** — the flaw the protagonist starts with
2. **Need** — what they don't yet know they need
3. **Desire** — what they want
4. **Plan** — how they'll get desire
5. **Battle** — opposition (antagonist shares same goal, opposite method per L008)
6. **Self-revelation** — they see their moral weakness
7. **New equilibrium** — moral correction OR refusal-to-correct (tragedy)

Use as a checklist — every iter file should be able to fill all 7 slots.

## §I — STEPPS Framework (Berger 2013)

Jonah Berger's 6-factor framework for what propagates virally. Score each concept on each axis (1-5):

- **Social Currency** — does sharing the concept make the sharer look interesting?
- **Triggers** — does the concept link to everyday cues that activate recall?
- **Emotion** — does the concept generate high-arousal emotion (anger, awe, anxiety)?
- **Public** — is the concept visible/discussable in public spaces?
- **Practical Value** — does engaging offer useful information or perspective?
- **Stories** — is the concept itself a story worth retelling?

**EMPIRICAL FINDING:** Social Currency × Emotion is the strongest two-factor predictor of viral propagation (Berger 2013). Concepts must score ≥3/5 on BOTH to have viral potential. Examples that pass: *Get Out* (social-justice conversation + fear), *Joker* (incel discourse + anger), *Squid Game* (debt anxiety + dread).

## §J — SUCCESs Framework (Heath 2007)

Chip & Dan Heath's 6-factor "stickiness" framework for ideas that survive transmission:

- **Simple** — core idea expressible in ≤10 words
- **Unexpected** — breaks pattern while remaining comprehensible
- **Concrete** — premise involves specific, imaginable situation
- **Credible** — internal logic convincing even if fantastical
- **Emotional** — stakes connected to universal human feelings
- **Story** — premise implies a narrative journey

Use as a logline-quality rubric. Concepts that fail Simple OR Concrete have a marketing ceiling regardless of execution (PM-D11/D12 violations).

## §K — Anti-Formulas (do NOT use)

The 8 attractor structures the v3.1 engine collapsed onto. The iterator MUST detect and refuse these. Encoded as `evals/test_idea_quality.py::test_no_template_frame`:

1. **"Seven unrelated lives in [Ethnicity] [City] during [Era] compose, between them, a single question…"** (mad-libs structure — 14 occurrences in v3.1 corpus)
2. **"[Archetype] in [setting] catalogues every variant of holding [abstract] against [abstract]…"** (mad-libs — 12 occurrences)
3. **"[Archetype] in [setting] is asked to translate between two communities for whom…"** (mad-libs — 12 occurrences)
4. **"[Archetype] discovers that [setting] is also a labyrinth in which…"** (mad-libs — 8 occurrences)
5. **AI-becomes-sentient** — pure training-data attractor
6. **Scientist who broke the rules** — pure training-data attractor
7. **Wise indigenous elder teaching the white protagonist** — colonial trope, training-data attractor
8. **Single tear in slow motion / In a world where…** — Hollywood marketing-genre cliché

If a draft falls into any of K.01-K.08, surface as a CRITICAL defect in self-critique and rewrite from formula-pick step.

## §L — Universal Invariants (the 6 patterns in BOTH commercial AND critical canon)

Per agent_3 §3.1. Every concept MUST satisfy all 6:

1. **Protagonist-Desire-Obstacle Triad** — articulated desire + structured obstacle
2. **Escalating Gap** (McKee) — growing space between expectation and result
3. **Universal Stakes** — survival, love, identity, justice, freedom, belonging, mortality
4. **Non-Trivial Transformation** — something changes (character, world, relationship, understanding)
5. **Moral / Philosophical Claim** — stateable as "if [value] leads to [action], then [consequence]"
6. **Cost-Bearing Conflict** — protagonist cannot win everything; price is genuine

Iterator self-check: each iter file must demonstrate all 6 in the 8-section structure.

## §O — Dual-Audience Sweet Spot (Barbie/Get Out/Parasite/Joker)

Per agent_4 §4.2 + agent_3 §3.7. The ~12% of $100M+ films that succeed at BOTH commercial AND critical levels share these 5 properties:

1. **Man-in-Hole arc** (B.01)
2. **Emotional contract satisfaction** (genre delivers what genre promises)
3. **Plot-mechanic subversion** (genre trope inverted, often at midpoint)
4. **Auteur-pre-authorized risk** (filmmaker's track record buys permission)
5. **Maintained genre clarity** (one-sentence pitch survives the subversion)

Cinema use: *Barbie* (toy comedy + feminist critique), *Get Out* (horror + race satire), *Parasite* (thriller + class commentary), *Joker* (origin movie + Scorsese character study), *Everything Everywhere All At Once* (multiverse action + immigrant family drama).

When all 5 hit, the concept is the highest-leverage commercial original.

## §Q — Cultural Discount Table (global reach)

Per agent_4 §4.6 (Hoskins & Mirus 1988):

| Genre | Cultural Discount | Global-reach friendliness |
|---|---|---|
| Action | LOW | HIGH (visual language universal) |
| Animation (kid-skewed) | LOW | HIGH |
| Drama | MEDIUM | MEDIUM |
| Horror | MEDIUM | MEDIUM (specific imagery travels) |
| Romance | MEDIUM | MEDIUM |
| Sci-Fi | LOW | HIGH |
| Comedy | HIGH | LOW (humor culturally specific) |
| Musical | HIGH | LOW |

When designing for global reach, prefer Action / Sci-Fi / Animation. When designing for cultural specificity (Korean Wave / Bollywood pattern), embed local texture in a globally-portable genre — this is the *Parasite* / *Squid Game* / *RRR* path.
