# Forced Collision — The COLLISION OPERATOR

## Frame

A "high-concept" film idea is not a premise that sounds clever in a pitch
room. It is an unlikely-but-coherent collision of four independently validated
components: a **validated psychological need** (SDT need from
`frameworks/sdt-spine.md`), an **untapped asset** (historical event, legal
document, scientific discovery, or cultural artifact that is in the public
domain or licensed, and that carries authentic emotional charge), an
**irreversibility pattern** (the specific action that can never be undone,
making the 90-minute clock narratively tractable), and a **TRIZ narrative
contradiction** (two poles the protagonist must hold simultaneously, both
stretched to breaking point).

The Anomaly Engine's PROJECT.md states the "audience deprivation thesis":
sustainable box-office interest is proportional to how vividly a film
addresses a systematic deprivation of an innate need — not vaguely, but
with the precision of a documentary frame on a lived scarcity. The
"Combinatorial multiplier" in PROJECT.md specifies that the collision must be
at the intersection of all four vectors simultaneously, not just two or three.
Any concept that passes only three of the four gates is incomplete and must
be iterated before scoring.

The COLLISION OPERATOR is the engine's core IP. An unguided LLM iterating
concept pitches explores the space of plausible combinations; the COLLISION
OPERATOR forces it into the narrow corridor of *unlikely-but-coherent*
combinations — the corridor where originality lives.

## The 12 TRIZ-Narrative Contradictions

Genrich Altshuller's TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving) holds that
every engineering breakthrough resolves a technical contradiction by
intensifying both poles simultaneously rather than compromising between them.
Applied to narrative, each contradiction below names two values the
protagonist must hold at the same time — values that pull in opposite
directions. The dramatic heat comes from the refusal to resolve the
contradiction by choosing one pole. The audience is kept in suspense because
neither pole is surrendered.

Resolution rule (Altshuller-style): to resolve the contradiction, intensify
*both* poles simultaneously to the point where a new structural solution
emerges that was invisible before the tension reached its peak.

---

### TC-01: Truth-vs-Belonging

**Definition:** The protagonist holds evidence of a truth that, if spoken,
will sever the social bond (family, tribe, class, nation) from which all
warmth and identity flow. Silence preserves belonging; speech is exile.
The contradiction lives in the fact that belonging without truth is
also a form of exile — from oneself.

Film exemplars:
- *Spotlight* (Tom McCarthy, 2015) — Reporters pursuing clerical abuse truth
  risk social ostracism from Boston's Catholic identity matrix.
- *The Report* (Scott Z. Burns, 2019) — Senate staffer Daniel Jones exposes
  CIA torture at the cost of institutional belonging and personal safety.
- *I, Daniel Blake* (Ken Loach, 2016) — The welfare claimant's honest
  declaration of incapacity challenges the bureaucratic fiction both sides
  are expected to maintain.

Resolution technique: force the protagonist to speak the truth *into* the
belonging structure (the church, the bureau, the family dinner) so that the
community must decide whether to crack or evolve.

---

### TC-02: Speed-vs-Care

**Definition:** The protagonist must act faster than the system allows for
careful action. Slowing down to be thorough means someone is harmed; moving
fast means acting without full information, risking a different harm.

Film exemplars:
- *The Hurt Locker* (Kathryn Bigelow, 2008) — EOD techs must defuse faster
  than human anxiety allows for care; slowing down may be lethal.
- *Contagion* (Steven Soderbergh, 2011) — Epidemiologists race a geometric
  viral curve; every day of scientific rigor costs thousands of lives.
- *Gravity* (Alfonso Cuaron, 2013) — Each careful repair action depletes
  oxygen the protagonist cannot afford; speed and precision fight each other
  inside a space suit.

Resolution technique: create a moment where the protagonist discovers that
speed and care are actually the same action — the "one precise move" that
is both fast and right, available only after the false binary has been
experienced at maximum intensity.

---

### TC-03: Loyalty-vs-Survival

**Definition:** Remaining loyal (to a person, a code, an institution) will
destroy the protagonist; abandoning loyalty is survival but self-annihilation
of a different kind. The contradiction is that loyalty is the protagonist's
identity — without it there is nothing to survive for.

Film exemplars:
- *The Deer Hunter* (Michael Cimino, 1978) — Michael returns to Vietnam to
  retrieve Nick; loyalty as suicidal obligation.
- *A Separation* (Asghar Farhadi, 2011) — Nader cannot both honor his
  bedridden father and his daughter's future; loyalty to one is betrayal
  of the other.
- *Carol* (Todd Haynes, 2015) — Therese's survival as an autonomous woman
  requires disloyalty to the social compact that defines her class position.

Resolution technique: find the version of loyalty that is also the truest
form of self-preservation — the action that redefines what survival means.

---

### TC-04: Power-vs-Innocence

**Definition:** The protagonist can only acquire the power necessary to
protect the innocent by becoming the kind of person who can no longer be
innocent. Every step toward effective power is a step away from the moral
clarity that made the goal worthwhile.

Film exemplars:
- *Sicario* (Denis Villeneuve, 2015) — Kate Macer's moral innocence becomes
  an operational liability; the system uses her innocence as cover and then
  discards it.
- *Zero Dark Thirty* (Kathryn Bigelow, 2012) — Maya's single-minded pursuit
  of bin Laden requires inhabiting a moral universe she cannot survive
  long-term.
- *Oldboy* (Park Chan-wook, 2003) — The protagonist's search for the power
  to answer his imprisonment requires actions that destroy the innocence
  of everyone he loves.

Resolution technique: have the protagonist surrender a specific form of
power (institutional, physical) in order to retain a specific form of
innocence — demonstrating that the two are not globally contradictory
but locally separable at a single decisive moment.

---

### TC-05: Memory-vs-Identity

**Definition:** The protagonist's sense of self depends on a memory that
investigation reveals to be false or incomplete. Preserving the false memory
preserves a coherent identity; accepting the truth requires dismantling who
they believed themselves to be.

Film exemplars:
- *Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind* (Michel Gondry, 2004) — Memory
  erasure as both identity destruction and a failed attempt at identity
  preservation.
- *Memento* (Christopher Nolan, 2000) — Leonard's identity is manufactured
  from tattooed evidence; the investigation of memory *is* the identity threat.
- *The Shining* (Stanley Kubrick, 1980) — Jack's recovered memory of "always
  having been the caretaker" dissolves his identity as a father and writer.

Resolution technique: give the protagonist a memory that is simultaneously
false as fact and true as feeling — so that accepting the factual falsity
does not require surrendering the felt identity it anchored.

---

### TC-06: Mercy-vs-Justice

**Definition:** The just resolution of the protagonist's conflict requires
an action that destroys someone who does not deserve destruction; the merciful
action perpetuates an injustice that also destroys someone else. There is no
option that does not leave blood on both sides of the scale.

Film exemplars:
- *The Lives of Others* (Florian Henckel von Donnersmarck, 2006) — Wiesler
  covers for the playwright; mercy to the artist, injustice to the system,
  yet the system is the injustice.
- *Sophie's Choice* (Alan J. Pakula, 1982) — The literal form of the
  contradiction: mercy to one child is death to the other.
- *No Country for Old Men* (Joel and Ethan Coen, 2007) — Bell cannot bring
  Chigurh to justice without becoming a version of Chigurh; merciful
  retirement preserves his soul at the cost of all future victims.

Resolution technique: force the protagonist to choose mercy over justice
in a single, specific instance — and then live with all downstream consequences
at full narrative weight.

---

### TC-07: Hope-vs-Evidence

**Definition:** The rational reading of all available evidence points to
certain failure; the protagonist acts on hope anyway. The contradiction is
that acting rationally (accepting defeat) is also irrational (because the
situation is already lost if you accept it).

Film exemplars:
- *Dunkirk* (Christopher Nolan, 2017) — Every decision on the beach defies
  the available evidence about survival probability.
- *The Pursuit of Happyness* (Gabriele Muccino, 2006) — Chris Gardner
  pursues a paid internship he has no rational basis for obtaining.
- *Interstellar* (Christopher Nolan, 2014) — Cooper bets humanity's survival
  on a wormhole whose existence the evidence only barely supports.

Resolution technique: show evidence changing as a consequence of the hopeful
action — so that the protagonist was not irrational but was acting on a
category of evidence (relational, intuitive) that instrumental analysis
could not capture.

---

### TC-08: Family-vs-Vocation

**Definition:** The protagonist's calling — what they were made to do —
requires absence, risk, or transformation that the family bond cannot survive.
Fulfilling the vocation destroys the family; sustaining the family destroys
the vocation. Both are forms of love.

Film exemplars:
- *Whiplash* (Damien Chazelle, 2014) — Andrew's pursuit of drumming
  greatness systematically dismantles every human relationship.
- *Tootsie* (Sydney Pollack, 1982) — Dorsey's acting vocation requires
  a transformation (gender performance) that reshapes all his attachments.
- *Portrait of a Lady on Fire* (Celine Sciamma, 2019) — The painter's
  vocation and the subject's love are inseparable and mutually fatal.

Resolution technique: reveal that the vocation and the family are expressing
the same need (to be seen, to be real) — and that one act can satisfy both
even as it ends one of the forms.

---

### TC-09: Faith-vs-Reason

**Definition:** The protagonist's received belief system and their
empirically-derived understanding of the world give directly contradictory
instructions about what to do. Acting on faith looks irrational from the
outside; acting on reason feels like betrayal from the inside.

Film exemplars:
- *Silence* (Martin Scorsese, 2016) — Rodrigues must apostatize (reason says
  surrender to save lives) or maintain faith (which condemns others to death).
- *The Mission* (Roland Joffe, 1986) — Mendoza chooses armed resistance over
  Father Gabriel's pacifist faith; two forms of the same devotion.
- *First Reformed* (Paul Schrader, 2017) — Toller's ecological despair
  dismantles the theological framework that gave his suffering meaning.

Resolution technique: find the single act in which the distinction between
faith and reason collapses — where the most rational thing *is* the leap
of faith, or vice versa.

---

### TC-10: Self-vs-Service

**Definition:** The protagonist's authentic development requires prioritizing
the self (desires, boundaries, ambitions); their commitment to service requires
systematic subordination of the self. Each choice deepens the debt to the
other side.

Film exemplars:
- *Parasite* (Bong Joon-ho, 2019) — The Kim family's self-advancement
  requires increasingly complete performance of service; the contradiction
  explodes when the servant in the basement appears.
- *Gran Torino* (Clint Eastwood, 2008) — Walt's self-preservation instinct
  and his service to Thao are resolved only by the sacrifice that destroys
  the self that was doing the serving.
- *A Beautiful Mind* (Ron Howard, 2001) — Nash's mathematical genius (a form
  of obsessive self-service) must be redirected into teaching and human
  connection (service) as the only therapeutic path.

Resolution technique: find the act of service that *is* self-expression —
the version of self that can only be articulated through giving it away.

---

### TC-11: Pleasure-vs-Meaning

**Definition:** The route to meaning is arid, costly, and stripped of hedonic
reward; the route to pleasure produces a life that feels empty from the
inside. The protagonist who chooses meaning looks depressed from the outside;
the one who chooses pleasure looks superficial from the inside.

Film exemplars:
- *Lost in Translation* (Sofia Coppola, 2003) — Bob and Charlotte have
  achieved all the pleasures their class provides; the Tokyo interlude is
  a search for meaning that pleasure cannot provide.
- *The Big Lebowski* (Joel and Ethan Coen, 1998) — The Dude's hedonist
  philosophy is both absurdist pleasure and a form of meaning-resistance.
- *American Beauty* (Sam Mendes, 1999) — Lester's late-life pleasure-seeking
  is actually a search for the meaning that his comfortable life extinguished.

Resolution technique: make the protagonist discover that the pleasurable
activity they dismissed as meaningless was, in fact, their highest form of
meaning — misread because meaning does not look like effort.

---

### TC-12: Freedom-vs-Belonging

**Definition:** Full personal freedom requires detachment from the social
matrix that gives life its warmth and continuity; full belonging requires
subordination of the self to the group's expectations in ways that foreclose
personal freedom. The freer the individual, the more alone; the more belonging,
the less free.

Film exemplars:
- *Into the Wild* (Sean Penn, 2007) — McCandless's radical freedom from
  social attachment ends in death; belonging would have saved him.
- *Nomadland* (Chloe Zhao, 2020) — Fern's freedom on the road is authentic
  and costly; the film refuses to resolve the contradiction by making
  either choice more heroic.
- *Marriage Story* (Noah Baumbach, 2019) — Charlie and Nicole must each
  discover which version of freedom is worth the rupture of belonging.

Resolution technique: show freedom and belonging as compatible in one
specific register (artistic, spiritual, parental) even as they remain
incompatible in the register (geographical, institutional) where the conflict
has been fought.

## The 12 Irreversibility Patterns

An irreversibility pattern is the specific action that, once taken, cannot
be undone — the narrative gate through which the protagonist passes with no
return ticket. Irreversibility is the structural engine of tension. The
audience waits for the irreversible act and experiences the remainder of the
film as aftermath. The 90-Minute Clock (see below) makes the irreversibility
tractable by anchoring it in clock time.

Rule of irreversibility: identify the precise moment when the protagonist
crosses the threshold. Everything before is set-up; everything after is
consequence. The irreversibility must be public, physical, or documentary —
something that leaves a trace in the world outside the protagonist's psychology.

---

### IP-01: Death

**Definition:** A character dies as a direct consequence of the protagonist's
action (or inaction). The death is irrevocable; the relationship, obligation,
or dynamic that existed before is permanently extinguished.

Film exemplars:
- *Requiem for a Dream* (Darren Aronofsky, 2000) — Sara Goldfarb's descent
  into electroshock is the irreversible consequence of a cascading set of
  enabling actions by everyone around her.
- *Incendies* (Denis Villeneuve, 2010) — The mother's death in the framing
  story sends the children into a past from which the truth cannot return.

The action that cannot be undone: the death itself. All post-death scenes
are consequence, never reversal.

---

### IP-02: Public Statement

**Definition:** The protagonist makes a declaration — spoken at a public
gathering, broadcast, published, sworn under oath — that cannot be retracted
without destroying all credibility. The statement is the act.

Film exemplars:
- *All the President's Men* (Alan J. Pakula, 1976) — The front-page
  publication of the Watergate story is the public statement that cannot
  be unpublished.
- *Frost/Nixon* (Ron Howard, 2008) — Nixon's admission on live television
  is the irreversible public statement; the interview cannot be un-broadcast.

The action that cannot be undone: the moment the microphone opens, the
camera rolls, or the print run ships.

---

### IP-03: Lost Document

**Definition:** A legal instrument, medical record, contract, letter, or
identity paper is destroyed, forged, or permanently lost. The documentary
reality it sustained is gone; all claims based on it are now unverifiable.

Film exemplars:
- *The Lives of Others* (Florian Henckel von Donnersmarck, 2006) — Wiesler's
  alteration of surveillance records creates a documentary gap that protects
  the artist but cannot be undone.
- *Chinatown* (Roman Polanski, 1974) — The water department's manipulated
  records are the lost document; the land ownership reality they distort
  cannot be recovered without them.

The action that cannot be undone: the moment the document is burned,
altered, or consumed in the shredder.

---

### IP-04: Pregnancy

**Definition:** A pregnancy begins. Whatever the ultimate outcome, the
biological fact is registered in the body and in the relationship. The
prior state — the uncomplicated dyad, the career path, the social position —
no longer exists.

Film exemplars:
- *Juno* (Jason Reitman, 2007) — The pregnancy is the engine; all subsequent
  choices (adoption plan, Mark and Vanessa, relationship with Paulie) are
  consequence.
- *4 Months, 3 Weeks and 2 Days* (Cristian Mungiu, 2007) — The pregnancy
  under Romanian communism is the total irreversibility; every scene is
  the attempt to navigate a situation that cannot be navigated safely.

The action that cannot be undone: the conception, confirmed by the test.

---

### IP-05: Crossing a Border

**Definition:** The protagonist enters a territory — physical, legal, or
social — from which they cannot return without catastrophic cost or explicit
permission. The border is real: a national frontier, a prison gate, a
criminal threshold, a career point of no return.

Film exemplars:
- *No Man's Land* (Danis Tanovic, 2001) — The trench is the border; both
  sides are trapped in the no-man's-land between them.
- *Midnight Express* (Alan Parker, 1978) — The Istanbul airport customs
  corridor is the border; the crossing is the irreversible act.

The action that cannot be undone: the moment the border is crossed with
no return path visible.

---

### IP-06: Killing the Messenger

**Definition:** The person who holds the information that could resolve
the protagonist's problem is silenced — through literal death, legal
injunction, social destruction, or credibility erasure — before the
information is transmitted.

Film exemplars:
- *Zodiac* (David Fincher, 2007) — Each potential witness is silenced,
  leaving the investigator with fragmentary evidence that cannot be made
  whole.
- *The Insider* (Michael Mann, 1999) — Wigand's legal silencing is the
  attempted messenger-killing; the CBS broadcast is the attempt to reverse it.

The action that cannot be undone: the silencing of the one person who knew.

---

### IP-07: Burned Bridge

**Definition:** The protagonist destroys a relationship, alliance, or
institution that cannot be rebuilt. The destruction is active: a confrontation,
a betrayal revealed, a resignation under hostile fire, a public humiliation.

Film exemplars:
- *There Will Be Blood* (Paul Thomas Anderson, 2007) — Plainview's final
  rejection of his son, declared as a public disowning, is the burned bridge
  from which there is no return.
- *The Social Network* (David Fincher, 2010) — Each lawsuit-triggering
  betrayal is a burned bridge; the Winklevoss settlement closes the door
  on any collaborative future.

The action that cannot be undone: the act of public severance.

---

### IP-08: Sealed Container

**Definition:** The protagonist (and possibly others) is enclosed — in a
physical space, a legal structure, or an information blackout — from which
exit requires external action that cannot be guaranteed. The sealing is the
irreversible act; the drama is the attempt to achieve release.

Film exemplars:
- *127 Hours* (Danny Boyle, 2010) — The canyon seals Aron Ralston; the arm
  is the second irreversibility nested inside the first.
- *Phone Booth* (Joel Schumacher, 2002) — Stu Shepard is sealed inside the
  booth by the sniper's logic; exiting costs more than staying.

The action that cannot be undone: the moment the container closes and
external release becomes the only exit condition.

---

### IP-09: Spoken Vow

**Definition:** The protagonist makes a verbal commitment — a promise, an
oath of loyalty, a marriage vow, a deathbed pledge — in a context where
breaking it carries catastrophic moral, legal, or social cost. The vow is
the act; all subsequent events test whether it can be kept.

Film exemplars:
- *Breaking Bad* (Vince Gilligan, series) — Walt's promise to himself that
  this is temporary and controlled is the spoken vow; the drama is its
  systematic destruction.
- *A Beautiful Day in the Neighborhood* (Marielle Heller, 2019) — The vow
  of friendship, spoken by Rogers and modeled for Lloyd, cannot be walked back
  without catastrophic moral cost.

The action that cannot be undone: the utterance of the vow in front of
a witness.

---

### IP-10: Made Public

**Definition:** Information that was private — a secret, a medical diagnosis,
an identity, a financial position — is released into a public sphere from
which it cannot be recalled. The protagonist must now live in a world that
knows something they cannot un-know.

Film exemplars:
- *Brokeback Mountain* (Ang Lee, 2005) — Alma's accidental discovery makes
  private love public knowledge inside the marriage; the secret is the engine.
- *The Imitation Game* (Morten Tyldum, 2014) — Turing's homosexuality, once
  exposed, cannot be re-privatized within the legal and social context of
  1950s Britain.

The action that cannot be undone: the moment the private fact enters
irreversibly public space.

---

### IP-11: Fingerprint Match

**Definition:** Forensic, legal, or documentary evidence definitively
identifies the protagonist (or a character whose identification changes
everything) as the agent of a prior act. The match is irreversible: it
connects a present identity to a past action that cannot be denied.

Film exemplars:
- *Prisoners* (Denis Villeneuve, 2013) — The forensic trail that links
  Alex Jones to the girls' disappearance is the fingerprint match driving
  the escalating interrogation.
- *Catch Me If You Can* (Steven Spielberg, 2002) — Each identity fraud is
  a fingerprint match waiting to be made; the dramatic irony is that the
  audience knows what Frank does not.

The action that cannot be undone: the match itself — the moment the
identity is confirmed by a record that pre-exists any attempt to destroy it.

---

### IP-12: Genie Out of Bottle

**Definition:** The protagonist releases a force, idea, technology, or
biological agent that, once released, propagates autonomously and cannot
be recalled. The protagonist is the initiating agent but not the controlling
agent of what follows.

Film exemplars:
- *Jurassic Park* (Steven Spielberg, 1993) — The dinosaurs are the genie;
  the park's systems attempted to keep them bottled; the storm uncorks them.
- *Ex Machina* (Alex Garland, 2015) — Caleb's liberation of Ava is the
  uncorking; the film's final images are the genie in the world without
  a bottle.

The action that cannot be undone: the moment of release — the gate opened,
the file uploaded, the culture inoculated.

## The 90-Minute Clock

The 90-Minute Clock is the narrative device that transforms abstract
irreversibility into felt urgency. Taken from David Bordwell's analysis
of Hollywood causality (Bordwell, *Narration in the Fiction Film*, Chapter 8:
"Classical Narration"), the 90-minute clock is the embedding of every
irreversibility pattern into a specific, ticking temporal window. The
audience experiences time as a resource being consumed. Once the clock is
visible — whether explicit (the bomb display), institutional (the court
deadline), biological (the window before infection spreads), or physical
(the storm that will close the pass at dawn) — every scene becomes a
transaction against an account that is running out.

Gerard Genette's analysis of temporal order, frequency, and duration
(*Narrative Discourse: An Essay in Method*, 1980) provides the theoretical
underpinning. Genette's *Duration* analysis shows that narrative compression
and expansion of screen time relative to story time produces emotional
emphasis. The 90-Minute Clock exploits Duration: the moments before the
irreversibility is reached are given maximum screen time (expansion);
the aftermath compresses rapidly. The ratio of pre-to-post clock time signals
to the audience precisely when the point of no return has passed.

In the COLLISION OPERATOR, every irreversibility pattern (IP-01 through
IP-12) must be bound to a calendar or clock anchor at STEP 3. This anchor
is not decoration — it is the structural mechanism that makes the
contradiction (TC-01 through TC-12) narratively tractable by giving the
protagonist a finite window in which to act. The 90-Minute Clock does not
have to be exactly ninety minutes of story time; it is the *felt* duration
of inevitability that the narrative produces, calibrated by Genette's Duration
ratio. For practical Forge use: specify the anchor (emergency response window,
judicial deadline, ice-shelf melt schedule, broadcast window, visa expiry)
and the engine will embed the constraint into the concept's spine.

## The COLLISION OPERATOR Algorithm

The algorithm below is illustrative pseudocode for the Forge LLM. It is
not executable Python. The actual orchestration lives in `pipeline/run.py`
(P3). ADR-0005 forbids importing from `frameworks/` in any Python module;
this pseudocode is read-only doctrine for the LLM, not importable code.

```
INPUT: (asset, sdt_primary_need, irreversibility_pattern_id, triz_contradiction_id)

Where:
  asset                    = {id, title, domain, historical_date, precedent_url}
  sdt_primary_need         = "autonomy" | "competence" | "relatedness"
  irreversibility_pattern_id = 1..12  (IP-01 through IP-12)
  triz_contradiction_id    = 1..12  (TC-01 through TC-12)

STEP 1 — ASSET VALIDATION:
  Verify asset has historical, scientific, or legal precedent.
  Emit: precedent_url (must be HEAD-checkable in P3 via scoring.py).
  Reject asset if precedent_url returns non-2xx and is not on allow-list.
  Emit: asset.domain_authenticity_flag = True if precedent confirmed.

STEP 2 — PROTAGONIST PREDICAMENT:
  Derive protagonist's "two-poles-stretched" predicament from contradiction.
  The predicament sentence takes the form:
    "[Protagonist] must [pole-A action] in order to [value-A],
     but [pole-A action] directly prevents [pole-B value], because [mechanism]."
  Apply Altshuller resolution rule: the resolution must intensify BOTH poles
  simultaneously; no compromise between them.
  Emit: forge_meta.predicament_sentence

STEP 3 — IRREVERSIBILITY + CLOCK ANCHOR:
  Bind irreversibility_pattern_id to a calendar or clock anchor.
  The clock anchor specifies:
    anchor_type: "deadline" | "biological" | "physical" | "institutional"
    anchor_window_hours: float  (the felt duration of the 90-Minute Clock)
    anchor_description: str
  Emit: forge_meta.clock_anchor

STEP 4 — COLLISION SIGNATURE:
  Emit: forge_meta.collision_signature = {
    asset_id:   asset.id,
    sdt_id:     sdt_primary_need,
    irrev_id:   irreversibility_pattern_id,
    triz_id:    triz_contradiction_id
  }
  The collision_signature must be unique vs. data/04_concepts.jsonl history
  (checked by pipeline/scoring.py::collision_signature_unique(signature, run_id)).

OUTPUT: forge_meta containing predicament_sentence, clock_anchor, collision_signature,
        precedent_url, asset.domain_authenticity_flag.
```

## Three Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Al-Bukhari (9th-century hadith verification x AI deepfakes)

**Asset:** Muhammad ibn Ismail al-Bukhari (810–870 CE) compiled the Sahih
al-Bukhari, the most authoritative collection of hadith in Sunni Islam, using
a rigorous chain-of-transmission verification system (isnad) that required
tracing every reported saying of the Prophet through a documented chain of
named, personally known, morally vetted narrators. The system was designed
to prevent fabrication. It was the most advanced fact-verification protocol
of the 9th century.

Precedent sources:
- Wikipedia: [en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_al-Bukhari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_al-Bukhari)
- Encyclopedia of Islam: [referenceworks.brillonline.com/browse/encyclopaedia-of-islam-2](https://referenceworks.brillonline.com/browse/encyclopaedia-of-islam-2)
- Seyyed Hossein Nasr, *The Study Quran* (HarperOne, 2015), introduction
  to hadith methodology.

**SDT primary need:** Relatedness. The audience's deprivation: in an AI
deepfake era, authentic testimony — the sense that a statement comes from
a real person with a real history — is systematically undermined. The viewer
craves the certainty that a witnessed statement is genuinely sourced. The
isnad system is precisely designed to restore that certainty; its collision
with AI fabrication makes the deprivation visceral.

**Irreversibility pattern:** IP-10 — Made Public. The AI-generated hadith
(in the concept's fictional world) has been broadcast on a major Islamic
media platform. Once broadcast, it cannot be un-broadcast. The protagonist
— a contemporary hadith scholar tasked with authentication — must operate
in a world where the fabrication is already in circulation.

**TRIZ contradiction:** TC-01 — Truth-vs-Belonging. The protagonist knows
the hadith is fabricated. If she speaks this truth, she fractures her
community's faith at a moment of maximum global tension. If she remains silent,
she belongs — but participates in a lie that is spreading with algorithmic
speed.

**STEP 1 — Asset validation:**
  precedent_url: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_al-Bukhari (returns 200)
  asset.domain_authenticity_flag: True (historical, documented, public domain)

**STEP 2 — Protagonist predicament:**
  "Dr. Amina Khalil must expose the fabricated hadith (truth) in order to
  protect the integrity of the transmission chain her life's work defends,
  but exposing it will split her community in a global media cycle, permanently
  altering the social fabric from which her identity as a scholar derives its
  meaning."
  Altshuller application: the resolution must intensify BOTH the truth-telling

  inventing a new verification protocol (an algorithmic isnad for AI outputs)
  that makes truth-telling and belonging-restoration the same act.

**STEP 3 — Irreversibility + Clock anchor:**
  anchor_type: institutional

  anchor_description: "The fabricated hadith was broadcast at 18:00 Thursday;
  Friday prayers begin globally 66-72 hours later; after sermons cite it,

  sdt_score reference: see `frameworks/sdt-spine.md` Al-Bukhari worked

**Concept one-liner:** A contemporary hadith scholar has 72 hours to

### Example 2 — Ostankino-1991 (couple trapped in elevator during tower fire)

**Asset:** The Ostankino TV Tower fire (August 27, 2000) — Moscow's 540-metre
broadcast tower caught fire in its cable shaft, killing 3 people and disabling
Russia's primary television broadcast infrastructure for months. The tower
was the sole broadcast point for national television at the moment of maximum
political instability (post-1998 financial crisis; Putin's first year in power).

Note on dating: the 1991 reference in the concept slug is a narrative device
— the concept is set during a hypothetical 1991 Ostankino tower crisis
(the August coup attempt, which did disrupt broadcasts) to maximize the
political stakes. The 2000 fire is the real-world asset; the fictional frame
maps it onto August 19-21, 1991 (the coup's 60-hour window).

Precedent sources:
- Wikipedia (2000 fire): [en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostankino_Tower_fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostankino_Tower_fire)
- Wikipedia (1991 coup): [en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_Soviet_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_Soviet_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat_attempt)
- Martin Sixsmith, *Russia: A 1,000-Year Chronicle of the Wild East*
  (Overlook Press, 2012), Chapter 27.

**SDT primary need:** Relatedness (primary), with Autonomy as secondary.
The audience's deprivation: in a surveillance culture, genuine private
intimacy — a conversation between two people that is truly unmonitored —
is systematically scarce. Two people trapped in an elevator inside a state
broadcast tower during a coup attempt are simultaneously the most surveilled
(KGB equipment throughout the tower) and the most truly isolated (fire has
cut the monitoring feeds) humans in the Soviet Union at that moment.

**Irreversibility pattern:** IP-08 — Sealed Container. The elevator cable
fails at the 337-metre level (the observation deck mechanical room). Smoke
rises from below; the elevator shaft is sealed by fire protocol. The couple
inside — a married woman who works in broadcast engineering and a dissident
journalist she was secretly meeting — are physically sealed. Calling for
rescue reveals her betrayal to her husband (who is the broadcast director
controlling the rescue operation from the ground floor).

**TRIZ contradiction:** TC-06 — Mercy-vs-Justice. The broadcaster's husband
controls the fire suppression system and the elevator emergency release. If
he activates the release, he saves his wife — an act of mercy — while
enabling her rescuer (the dissident) to escape and publish material that will
end the coup (justice for the nation). But rescuing the dissident is
simultaneously an act of injustice toward the coup's supporters — his own
career network. And revealing that his wife is in the elevator with the
dissident is an act of justice toward himself that destroys her.

**STEP 1 — Asset validation:**
  precedent_url: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostankino_Tower_fire
  asset.domain_authenticity_flag: True (documented, physical, public record)

**STEP 2 — Protagonist predicament:**
  "Yuri Morozov (broadcast director, ground floor) must activate the elevator
  release to save his wife (mercy), but the release mechanism is wired to a
  broadcast interlock that will simultaneously restore Channel One's feed —
  which his wife's contact has pre-loaded with a statement from Yeltsin that
  will end the coup (justice for the nation), and which Yuri was ordered by
  the coup committee to prevent from airing (injustice to his patrons)."
  Altshuller application: Yuri discovers that the mercy act (releasing the
  elevator) is also the justice act (enabling the broadcast) because the fire
  suppression system requires the same circuit to activate both simultaneously.

**STEP 3 — Irreversibility + Clock anchor:**
  anchor_type: physical + institutional (compound)
  anchor_window_hours: 3 (the elevator's emergency oxygen supply; also the
  broadcast window before the coup committee's technician arrives to manually
  cut the feed)
  anchor_description: "19 August 1991, 06:00. The window before the coup
  committee's broadcast engineer reaches the transmitter room to cut Channel
  One manually. After 09:00, the Yeltsin statement cannot air regardless
  of what Yuri does."

**STEP 4 — Collision signature:**
  collision_signature = {
    asset_id:  "ostankino-tower-1991-coup",
    sdt_id:    "relatedness",
    irrev_id:  8,
    triz_id:   6
  }

**Concept one-liner:** Inside Moscow's Ostankino broadcast tower on the
morning of the 1991 coup, a broadcast director has three hours to choose
whether to free an elevator that will simultaneously save his wife,
reveal her betrayal, and air the broadcast that ends the Soviet Union.

### Example 3 — Mamontenok (Soviet animation x Alaska immigrant kid x ice migration race)

**Asset:** *Mamontenok* (1981) — Soyuzmultfilm short animated film directed
by Roman Kachanov. Running time: 10 minutes. A baby mammoth wakes from
frozen ground in Siberia and searches for its mother, guided by a Yakut boy.
The film is a Soviet children's classic; its emotional engine is the primal
separation from kin — relatedness at maximum deprivation. The mammoth's
name is Dima. The film's final image (Dima finding its herd in a distant age)
is one of the most affecting endings in Soviet animation.

Precedent sources:
- Soyuzmultfilm official catalogue: [soyuzmultfilm.ru/cartoon/mamontenok](https://soyuzmultfilm.ru/cartoon/mamontenok)
- Wikipedia: [en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mamontenok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mamontenok)
- Michael Barrier, *Hollywood Cartoons: American Animation in Its Golden Age*
  (Oxford University Press, 1999) — comparative context for Soyuzmultfilm
  aesthetic tradition.

**SDT primary need:** Relatedness (kin separation). The audience's deprivation:
recent migration literature and audience-research studies document that
second-generation immigrant children experience the most severe form of
identity-split loneliness — belonging to no culture fully, carrying the
memory of a homeland they never actually inhabited. A Yupik Alaskan boy
whose grandparents relocated from Siberia is a literal inheritor of the
same migration that separated Dima from his herd. The asset is his.

**Irreversibility pattern:** IP-05 — Crossing a Border. The ice bridge
between the Siberian and Alaskan continental shelves — the Bering Land
Bridge — is the border. In the concept, a geological survey determines that
a viable permafrost mammoth specimen, identifiable via a GPS beacon embedded
by a Soviet-era research team, will become irrecoverably submerged as climate-
driven ice-shelf melt accelerates the land bridge's final submersion. The
protagonist (the Alaska boy, age 13) has one migration window to cross the
ice to the Siberian side before the bridge is permanently gone. Once it
submerges, the crossing can never be repeated. He will have either retrieved
the specimen (finding his heritage) or lost it to the sea.

**TRIZ contradiction:** TC-12 — Freedom-vs-Belonging. The boy's freedom —
to go, to claim the heritage, to be the one who crosses — requires that he
leave his Alaska village community (his American belonging) and cross to a
country he is not a citizen of. Full belonging to his Yupik identity requires
the crossing; but the crossing puts him outside every legal and social
structure that provides his physical safety. His grandmother crossed once,
in the other direction. He must cross back.

**STEP 1 — Asset validation:**
  precedent_url: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mamontenok
  asset.domain_authenticity_flag: True (documented Soviet animation;
  cultural heritage; Soyuzmultfilm catalogue entry verifiable)

**STEP 2 — Protagonist predicament:**
  "Alexei Nunapik must cross the ice bridge to retrieve the mammoth specimen
  (freedom: the act that claims his Yupik heritage and his grandmother's
  story as his own), but crossing requires abandoning his Alaska village
  — the belonging structure that is the only community he has ever physically
  inhabited — during the week of the village's Kivgiq (Messenger Feast), the
  ceremony whose attendance defines membership in the community."
  Altshuller application: the resolution comes when Alexei discovers that
  the Kivgiq itself is a crossing ritual — designed for the moment when a
  young person crosses into adult identity by retrieving something from the
  ancestral world. He is not abandoning the ceremony; he is performing it.

**STEP 3 — Irreversibility + Clock anchor:**
  anchor_type: physical (geological)
  anchor_window_hours: 96 (the USGS satellite-determined window before the
  ice-shelf fracture makes the crossing impossible; the beacon signal will
  be lost 4 hours after the ice submerges)
  anchor_description: "USGS model: the Bering Land Bridge permafrost at
  65.5 N will complete final submersion within a 96-hour window beginning
  Kivgiq morning. After the window closes, the ice crossing is structurally
  impossible for a minimum of 30 years — possibly forever."

**STEP 4 — Collision signature:**
  collision_signature = {
    asset_id:  "mamontenok-soyuzmultfilm-1981",
    sdt_id:    "relatedness",
    irrev_id:  5,
    triz_id:   12
  }

**Concept one-liner:** A 13-year-old Yupik boy in Alaska has 96 hours to
cross the last navigable section of the Bering Land Bridge to the Siberian
side before it submerges permanently — retrieving a Soviet-era mammoth
specimen and, with it, the only living proof that his grandmother's migration
story is real.

## Eno Oblique Strategies Hook

When the Forge stalls — defined as `forge_meta.seed_increments >= 2` (two
consecutive seeded attempts have produced concepts that fail the collision
signature uniqueness check or the TRIZ-distinctness gate) — the engine draws
one card at random from `pipeline/data/oblique_strategies.json`. The draw is
deterministic given the current run's seed, ensuring reproducibility. The
selected card's text is prepended to the Forge's next prompt as a lateral
constraint, forcing the LLM out of the attractor basin it has fallen into.

Brian Eno and Peter Schmidt's Oblique Strategies (first edition 1975; fifth
edition 2001) are a set of aphoristic prompts designed to break creative
impasse by imposing an unexpected constraint. The cards are used by composers,
filmmakers, and writers as anti-pattern interrupts. The engine uses them as
exactly that: the card is not the concept, it is the interrupt. After the
interrupt, the Forge re-runs the COLLISION OPERATOR with the same four input
vectors (asset, sdt, irrev, triz) but constrained by the card's lateral
instruction (e.g., "Use an unacceptable color," "Emphasize the flaws," "Work
at a different speed").

The full card set lives in `pipeline/data/oblique_strategies.json` (created
in plan 01-04). The framework file does not duplicate the cards; it only
describes the invocation protocol. The JSON key is `cards: [{id, text}]`;
the draw function is `random.choice(cards, seed=run_seed + seed_increment)`.

This mechanism is the engine's anti-homogeneity control. Without it, the
Forge with a fixed prompt template will converge on a small set of
high-probability collision patterns after K=3 or K=4 iterations. The Eno
interrupt rotates the attractor basin, maintaining diversity across a long
run (--n 100 or more) without human intervention.

## Operational Integration

This section is the canonical interface between `forced-collision.md` and
the rest of the Anomaly Engine pipeline. All fields listed here are produced
by the Forge and consumed by the Critic and the Scoring layer.

### Forge Fields Produced

The COLLISION OPERATOR emits the following fields into `forge_meta`:

| Field | Type | Values | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| `collision.triz_id` | int | 1..12 | TRIZ contradiction chosen (TC-01..12) |
| `collision.irrev_id` | int | 1..12 | Irreversibility pattern chosen (IP-01..12) |
| `collision.precedent_url` | str | URL | HEAD-checkable in P3 via scoring.py |
| `collision.asset_id` | str | identifier | Asset slug from concept brief |
| `collision.collision_signature` | dict | {asset_id, sdt_id, irrev_id, triz_id} | composite uniqueness key |
| `collision.predicament_sentence` | str | max 60 words | two-poles-stretched predicament |
| `collision.clock_anchor` | dict | {type, hours, description} | 90-Minute Clock binding |
| `collision.asset_domain_authenticity` | bool | True/False | STEP 1 output |

### Critic Re-checks

The Critic agent (P5) independently verifies:

1. `precedent_url` returns 2xx or is on the `ALLOW_LISTED_BOT_BLOCK_HOSTS`
   list (checked by `pipeline/scoring.py::url_reachable(url) -> bool`).
2. `collision_signature` is unique vs. all prior concepts in
   `data/04_concepts.jsonl` for the current run (not a re-collision).
3. `predicament_sentence` instantiates both poles of the named TRIZ
   contradiction (not just one pole).
4. `clock_anchor.hours` is plausible for the stated `anchor_type` (a
   biological clock cannot be 0.5 hours; an institutional deadline cannot
   be 10 years).
5. `sdt_id` matches the sdt-spine.md `sdt_score` calculation for the
   asset (cross-reference check).

### P3 Scoring Target

```
pipeline/scoring.py::collision_signature_unique(signature: dict, run_id: str) -> bool
```

Returns `True` if the `(asset_id, sdt_id, irrev_id, triz_id)` tuple has not
appeared in `data/04_concepts.jsonl` for the given `run_id`. The uniqueness
check prevents the Forge from re-discovering the same collision in a long run.

### Cross-References

- `frameworks/sdt-spine.md` — The `sdt_primary_need` field (INPUT 2 of 4)

  whether the asset genuinely addresses the chosen SDT need. A concept with
