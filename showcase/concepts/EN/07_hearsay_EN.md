# Hearsay

#### Logline
When a precedent-setting murder trial hinges on the testimony of a deaf woman who once pulled her from a burning car, the court's sign-language interpreter — who has quietly softened that woman's words for years — must decide whether to translate the one sentence that will free her and detonate every relationship the interpreter has, or finally surrender her grip on the truth and let the silenced speak for herself.

#### Tagline
The truth has to pass through someone. What if she changes it to save you?

## Revenue Thesis

The right tier here is the contained, dialogue-driven thriller built around one extraordinary performance — the economic profile of *Glass* or *Ghost*, not a visual-effects tentpole. The genre rewards discipline: a single courthouse, a handful of rooms, and a cast led by craft rather than spectacle keeps negative cost low and lets the multiple do the work.

## Why Now

Courtrooms have become the culture's chosen stage for arguments about who gets to be believed, and the question of whether a voice that passes through an intermediary — an interpreter, a translator, a captioning feed, an algorithm — is still that person's own voice has moved from the margins to the center of public life. Audiences have shown an appetite for grounded, performance-led moral dramas that turn a single ethical knot, and for stories that put Deaf experience and sign language on screen as something cinematic rather than incidental. The half-second between a witness's hands and an interpreter's mouth is suddenly the most charged silence in any room — and no film has yet planted its camera inside it.

# 2. The Concept

## Mass-Appeal Theme

The human truth underneath is one everyone has lived in miniature: love is not speaking *for* the people we want to protect — it is handing them back their own voice and surviving what they choose to say with it. The story carries that truth out to its hardest edge through a woman whose entire gift is making other people's pain bearable, cornered at last by the one truth she cannot soften.

The genre convention it breaks is vindication. A courtroom thriller is built to deliver the moment the hidden truth comes out and the righteous side wins. Here the prosecutor is not corrupt — he is correct. The protagonist's victory is not exposure but self-incrimination. And the climactic move is not seizing the witness chair but surrendering it. The suspense is ethical, not whodunit; the relief, when it comes, is earned and devastating rather than triumphant.

## Format & Genre

This belongs on the big screen as a feature because its entire engine is a single performance held under unbearable pressure — silence, hands, and the held second before a mouth moves — the kind of contained, escalating ordeal a theatrical run and an awards platform are built to reward. It is a chamber piece by design, and the constraint is the commercial asset.

- **Genre:** Courtroom chamber-thriller in the key of a grief drama
- **Runtime:** 115–125 minutes
- **Budget Tier:** Contained / single-location (~$20–35M negative cost, *Glass* / *Ghost* lane)

## Tonal Contract

A taut courtroom chamber-thriller in the key of a grief drama — the procedural tension of a precedent trial fused with the intimate ache of an unpayable debt. Grounded, hushed, sensory: the click of the booth mic, the rhythm of hands, the half-second lag between sign and speech where the whole film lives. Closer to a moral pressure cooker than a twist machine; the suspense is ethical, not whodunit. No sentimentality and no villain — the prosecutor is right, which is what makes it hurt. The ending is earned, not happy.

# 3. Story

## Synopsis

MARA VANCE is the most requested sign-language interpreter in the state's courts, famous for keeping Deaf and non-speaking witnesses calm under cross-examination. Her secret skill is also her sin: in the booth, in the half-second between a witness's hands and her own mouth, she rounds the sharp edges off testimony — turning "I wanted him dead" into "I was afraid of him" — small mercies that have quietly held a dozen trials together. She tells herself she translates feeling, not just words. She is, by craft and by wound, a woman who edits other people's truth to make it survivable, and she has never once been caught.

The case that breaks her is a precedent test the state Supreme Court has agreed to decide by the end of the week — an unusually short fuse that hangs over every scene like a dropping blade. DEZI OKAFOR, Deaf since birth, stands accused of killing the man who trafficked her younger sister, AMARA, who is still missing and still in the trafficking network's reach; whether Amara can be pulled to safety depends on what this verdict does to the people protecting her. Dezi will testify only in sign. And Dezi is the woman who, eleven years ago, smashed a car window and dragged an unconscious Mara from a wreck before it burned — a debt Mara has carried like a held breath ever since. The prosecution, led by ADRIAN ROAN, has built its entire strategy on a question the law has never answered: when a witness speaks only through one interpreter, whose testimony is it? Mara petitions onto the case and lies on the conflict-of-interest form to get into the booth. She visits Dezi in holding and secretly coaches which signs to use. She destroys an old interpreter's notebook that proves she once softened a confession. Three deliberate, self-incriminating choices — all to win.

The inciting incident detonates on day three, with the court's deadline now two days out. Under oath, Dezi signs a sentence Mara has never let her finish before: "I killed him, and I would do it again, and you know why." It is the truth. It is also a confession that hands Roan his precedent and ends Dezi's life — and the clock on Amara's rescue runs out with it. The whole courtroom waits on Mara's mouth. She has one second to do what she has always done — to soften "would do it again" into "could not stop myself" — and she does it. Roan smells it instantly and subpoenas the booth recordings, the depositions, every trial where Mara's renderings drifted from the signed record. The more Mara grips, the worse it gets: each act of protective editing she has ever done becomes a thread Roan pulls, because every move she makes to control meaning proves his thesis — that Dezi was never speaking; Mara was. At the midpoint, Dezi — who has been reading Mara's lips and hands all along — signs to her in the holding cell: "I know what you do. I have always known. I have stayed silent for eleven years to keep your secret safe. Stop saving me." The debt runs both ways. The woman Mara is killing herself to rescue chose, long ago, to protect her in return — and now refuses to be spoken for, even if being heard is what dooms her.

The night before the ruling, Mara does the one thing she has never done off the record: she sits with her wife and tells the truth herself, privately, as a person and not a witness — not because she is caught, but because she is choosing to give it. Then, in open court, she collapses the two doors she has been trapped between — speak the true sentence and doom Dezi, or soften it forever and erase Dezi into a being who cannot speak for herself, proving Roan's precedent and dooming every silent witness to come. She refuses both. On the record she confesses to years of editing testimony, moves for her own voluntary disbarment, and submits to a forfeiture of every fee earned on the tainted trials as restitution to the witnesses she altered — her career, her marriage, her standing in the Deaf community gone, exactly as the wound foretold. Then she moves that Dezi testify directly to the jury in sign, with the transcript projected and three independent interpreters cross-checking, so that for the first time the meaning belongs to Dezi and not to Mara. By letting go of the booth — by surrender, not force — she does the one thing that actually frees Dezi: she makes her a witness in her own right. Dezi signs her own true, terrible, exonerating story; the jury, finally hearing HER, sees self-defense where the record alone saw murder; and the protective network, with the verdict landing in time, reaches Amara before the network does. The precedent the court hands down is not Roan's cruel one — "the silent have no testimony" — but its inversion: the silent must be given more than one voice, never just one. The unrepayable debt is repaid not by Mara saving Dezi's life, but by giving back the voice she had been borrowing for eleven years.

## Emotional Arc

The film opens on quiet competence curdling into dread — a woman whose gift is making other people's pain bearable, cornered by the one truth she cannot soften and a clock she cannot stop. It rises through escalating control and panic, each rescue attempt sinking her deeper, to the gut-drop midpoint where the person she is dying to save reveals she has been protecting Mara all along and says, "stop saving me." It falls to the lowest point — the private confession to her wife, then the public one, the loss of every relationship, the wound made fully real. Then it turns, not into triumph, but into a clean, devastating release: the held breath of eleven years let go. The single image meant to make a viewer cry in memory is Mara stepping back from the interpreter's booth and going still — hands lowered, mouth closed — while Dezi, lit alone, lifts her own hands and tells the jury her terrible true story for the first time, and is finally, completely heard.

# 4. Characters

## Protagonist

**Mara Vance** — a court interpreter sworn to render every word exactly, who became the best in the state precisely because she cannot stop privately editing other people's pain into something survivable. The same mercy-instinct that makes her trusted is the lie that will destroy her.

- **Want:** to control the testimony — to choose the exact words that will acquit Dezi, the woman who once saved her life, and so finally repay an unrepayable debt.
- **Need:** to let go of being the gatekeeper of other people's truth, and accept that a life is repaid not by saving it but by handing back the voice you have been speaking for.

The role is a single virtuosic instrument: silence, hands, and the held second before the mouth moves. The drama plays out in micro-expression and the lag between a witness's sign and a chosen word — an awards-and-star vehicle of the kind that decides whether a contained chamber piece reaches the upper half of its range or its floor.

## Antagonist

**The Standard of Verbatim**, embodied in prosecutor **Adrian Roan** and the precedent itself — a state Supreme Court test asking whether interpreted testimony from a non-speaking witness can ever count as the witness's own words. Roan is not evil; he is correct. He argues that a witness who speaks only through one interpreter has no testimony at all — only the interpreter's. If Mara can shade a single word, then no Deaf, mute, or foreign witness is ever truly heard in a courtroom.

His rightness has a wound of its own. Years earlier, as a young prosecutor, Roan won a conviction built on translated testimony — and the wrong man went to prison because the interpreter, not the witness, had quietly chosen the damning word. The man died inside before the error surfaced. Roan's crusade for a clean, citable rule is not abstraction; it is penance. He optimizes for one thing: that meaning belongs to the record, not to the messenger — even if the rule erases the only person who ever made the silenced audible. The system's mercy and the system's honesty cannot both survive, and Roan has buried a man under the difference.

## Key Characters

**Dezi Okafor** — Deaf since birth, accused of killing the trafficker who took her sister. The woman who pulled Mara from a burning car eleven years ago, and who has read every word off Mara's lips and hands ever since. Her silence is not passivity; it is an eleven-year gift she chose, staying quiet to protect Mara's secret. She refuses to be spoken for, even when being heard is what dooms her — making the debt run both ways and the rescue a thing she must take back into her own hands.
