# Conveyance

#### Logline
When a hospice nurse returns to empty and sell the family house she grew up in, the rooms begin replaying one winter night across three decades — and force her to discover that the sister she has mourned for thirty years as her savior was the child her own frightened lie left out in the cold.

#### Tagline
She always knew who saved her. She never asked who she left behind.

## Revenue Thesis

The budget tier is mid-range prestige limited series — the band that funds a single contained location rendered with obsessive period fidelity (one house, lovingly different across 1971, 1991, and 2009) rather than scale spectacle, putting every dollar on faces, light, and craft. The comps justify the multiple. *Gravity* (2013) converted a $105M budget into $723.2M worldwide for a roughly 5.9x return ([Box Office Mojo](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/title/tt1454468/)), proving that a single protagonist trapped in a confined space, fighting to survive an ordeal the audience never leaves, is a commercial engine, not an arthouse constraint. *Cinderella* (2015) turned $95M into $543.5M worldwide for a 4.7x return ([Box Office Mojo](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/title/tt1661199/)), demonstrating that a wholly familiar domestic story, re-shot with prestige restraint and a clean emotional through-line, crosses every demographic and every border. *Home* (2015) returned a more modest 1.7x on $135M ([Box Office Mojo](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/title/tt2224026/)) — included honestly as the softest performer in the set, a reminder that the family-and-memory register travels but caps its upside when the hook is gentle rather than gripping; *Threshold* answers that risk with a thriller spine and a reversal that *Home* never carried.

On this comp corridor, a mid-tier limited-series license supports a base-case investor multiple in the low-to-mid single digits against the platform fee, with breakeven achievable inside the first license window and the bulk of upside arriving in renewal, second-window, and the anthology architecture described below. The closed, devastating ending is the asset: it makes the season fully self-contained IP a platform can buy without franchise commitment, while the same-house anthology engine preserves the option to extend.

## Why Now

Audiences have moved past the cozy ghost story and the tidy reckoning. The defining emotional current of the moment is the suspicion that the stories families tell about their own losses are edited — that the comforting version of a death protects the living at the dead's expense, and that grief mis-shelved for decades quietly deforms a life. A series whose horror is not a monster but accuracy — a house that simply refuses to let a woman keep lying about the worst night of her life — lands precisely on that nerve, and the appetite for restrained, performance-led prestige drama that treats the supernatural as memory with perfect recall rather than spectacle has rarely been stronger.

# 2. The Concept

## Mass-Appeal Theme

The human truth carried out is the most quietly universal one there is: that people love each other by lying about how they lost each other, and that the lie, however merciful, becomes its own slow grief. *Threshold* takes the comfort of the rescue story — *she died saving me, I am the lucky one* — and turns it into the wound, because the comfort was built on the dead sister's back. The series argues, scene by scene, that a debt which cannot be repaid is not paid and not forgiven but borne: that the only honest thing left to do with an irreversible loss is to witness it, unedited, for the rest of a life.

The genre convention it breaks is the spine of every haunted-house-and-buried-secret drama before it. The predictable beat is the descendant who excavates an ancestor's hidden sin — the protagonist as tour guide through other people's time. This inverts the entire mechanism: the woman walking the house is the one being excavated. She is not discovering a sin done *to* her family; she is discovering the sin *she* committed inside these walls as a nine-year-old, the lie she has spent thirty years remembering backward, certain she was the one rescued. The savior was the victim. The debt runs the other direction. No template generates that turn.

## Format & Genre

A limited series is the only shape that can hold this premise, because the engine is excavation: the house peels Edie's thirty-year edit back one layer at a time, one room and one decade per episode, and that controlled descent toward a single doorway needs the room a season provides and the discipline a closed ending demands. A feature would compress the slow correction of a lie into a twist; a series lets the audience live inside the mis-shelved grief long enough to feel it re-shelved.

- **Genre:** Prestige supernatural family drama with the spine of a cold-case investigation
- **Runtime:** 6 episodes, approximately 50–58 minutes each
- **Budget Tier:** Mid-range prestige limited series

## Tonal Contract

A patient, dread-soaked domestic chamber piece in the register of prestige limited drama — the moral undertow of *Atonement*, the restraint of *The Remains of the Day* — with the rooms-as-time mechanism handled as quiet, rule-bound, almost clinical strangeness rather than jump-scare horror. The supernatural is never explained and never fought; it behaves like memory with perfect recall, a house haunted not by spirits but by accuracy. The terror is recognition, not threat — emotionally devastating, never gory. Warm production-design intimacy, the same rooms lovingly different across three decades, set against the cold of one repeating winter night. The series earns one or two unbearable silences per episode, not spectacle, and the climax is a woman sitting with a death the way she has sat with a hundred strangers' — a hand laid over a hand, registering the cold, not a confession shouted into a storm.

# 3. Story

## Synopsis

Edith "Edie" Calloway, forty-nine, is the hospice nurse families request when someone is dying — the calmest hand at any deathbed, the one who can sit with the dying when no one else can bear to, who goes home each night to nowhere. She has a private creed, sharpened over thirty years and never once turned on herself: *you narrate every death into something witnessed.* When her mother dies, Edie returns alone to the clapboard house on Harrow Lane to empty it, settle the estate, and sell it — with a closing date already on the calendar, a buyer waiting, and a realtor counting days. She has not slept under this roof since she was nine, the winter her older sister Wren vanished one night and was found weeks later frozen in a culvert two fields east. The town story, the family story, Edie's own story, is settled and sacred: Wren died a hero, pushing little Edie back inside to safety before going out into the storm. Edie is the saved one. The lucky one. The daughter assigned, from that night forward, to be the steady one, the survivor — a role the house pinned on her and she has spent a lifetime refusing to come home and reckon with.

The house does not agree with the story. On her second night, Edie wakes to find the upstairs hall — gutted and repainted decades before she was born — restored to its 1971 wallpaper, with a child's wet bootprints leading to a door that swings shut on its own, exactly the way she has spent thirty years insisting it never did. Each room she enters resets to a different decade, but always the same night: the kitchen at 1991, the porch at 2009, the doorway at 1971, and in each, the night plays a little longer, a little less edited. She does the only thing her whole life has trained her for — she stays with the dying thing and makes herself watch — but she is not free to leave even if she wanted to: the estate cannot close until the recovered-remains question is answered, the buyer's clock is burning, and the house is the only place on earth where the unedited night still exists. Every replay she chooses to sit through spends a real day she does not have, and selling the house would erase the only record of what actually happened. She is bound by the sale, by her sister's bones, and by the one compulsion she has built a career on. So Edie investigates. She *pulls* the sealed 1971 county sheriff's file from the archive and cross-references its search-grid map against the geography the rooms keep replaying. She *stations* herself inside the 1971 doorway scene like a nurse at a bedside, refusing to look away. And she *drives* to the care facility and confronts the elderly deputy who took her childhood statement.

For half the season the evidence points away from her. The deputy's notes, the 1991 kitchen replay, and a cousin's old account all seem to converge on the mother — that it was their grief-wrecked mother who told the search party Wren ran east, who steered the men into the wrong field, who carried the family's guilt and drank on it for twenty years. Edie builds the case against her mother and half-believes it; the audience builds it with her. Then the doorway forecloses it. The rooms play the night without anyone's edits, and the discrepancy resolves the wrong way: the search looked east because a nine-year-old, fluent and fast and terrified she'd be blamed for letting Wren out to chase the dog into the snow, told the deputy her sister ran east — a lie Edie has no memory of telling. She sees her own small hand pull the door shut on her sister's voice. Wren did not save Edie. Edie shut the door, sent the searchers the wrong way, and survived on the credit of a sister she spent. The savior was the victim. The debt runs backward — and the worst grief of her life is the discovery that she mourned wrongly for thirty years.

Then the vise doubles. The cousin who knew the family, shown only a fragment of what Edie has found, draws exactly the wrong conclusion — that Wren must have run off on her own that night — and thanks Edie, with tears, for "surviving it all so gracefully," for carrying the family with such steadiness. To accept the sympathy is to let Wren stay the runaway forever; to refuse it is to indict herself before she can bear to. Edie's instinct is to confess — to take the blame publicly at last, to *pay.* But the house, and Wren-in-the-replays, reject every confession she rehearses, resetting to the doorway each time, because confession is just the apology version of the lie: another story that makes the night about Edie. In the final movement both poles she has lived between — *I was saved, owed an unpayable debt* and *I am guilty, and must finally pay* — collapse, revealed as two edits that keep the night about her. She does the third thing. Standing in the doorway one last time, she does what nine-year-old Edie could not: she opens the door and goes out into the replayed storm after Wren — not to rewrite the past, which is impossible, and not to die in penance, which is still self-serving, but to perform the one act she was actually built for and had spent her life giving only to strangers. She finds the replayed Wren in the culvert and stays. She lays her adult hand flat over child-Wren's, not to revive but to register the cold, and narrates the death into something witnessed instead of buried. The unpayable debt is neither paid nor forgiven; it is borne, in full daylight, unedited, for the rest of her life. The house releases. Edie does not sell it — she lives in it, finally a person who can be held by a room. She tells the town the true story, including the part where she lied and the field they should have searched. Wren's remains are recovered properly. The lucky daughter becomes, at last, the honest one.

## Emotional Arc

The series rises on brittle, professionally armored competence — a woman who can soothe any death but her own past — through a dawning dread as the house refuses her edits and the investigation seems, mercifully, to point at her mother. Then it drops through the trapdoor of the midpoint: the savior-story she built her entire identity on inverts, and she sees she was the cause, not the saved — a fall into the worst grief there is, the grief of having mourned wrongly for thirty years, deepened by the cousin's thanks that would trap Wren as a runaway forever. From that floor it climbs again, not to relief but to something heavier and truer: the hard-won peace of a person who stops bargaining for innocence or punishment and simply agrees to carry the real weight. The audience feels the universal ache of family grief mis-shelved — of loving someone by lying about how you lost them — and the strange release of finally telling the truth about a death no one could have prevented.

# 4. Characters

## Protagonist

**Edith "Edie" Calloway**, forty-nine, the hospice palliative nurse renowned for being the calmest hand at any deathbed — the one who can sit with the dying when no one else can bear to. Her inner contradiction is the engine of the series: she gives every stranger the steady, witnessing presence she has spent her whole life refusing to give the one room that made her, and she has weaponized her own creed — *you narrate every death into something witnessed* — against her own past, narrating a hundred deaths into peace while keeping her sister's buried and unspoken. **What she wants** is to empty the house, sell it, settle the estate, and finally close the door on the place that decided she was "the steady one, the lucky daughter" — to be done with being who the house made her. **What she needs** is to stop accepting the story that she was the one saved, and to face that she was nine years old in a doorway making a choice — that her whole adult calm is a thirty-year apology she never let herself say — and to replace an absolution she cannot earn with a truth she can finally carry.

## Antagonist

The antagonist is **the house as a recursive witness** — and, in human form, **Wren**, Edie's older sister, returning through the replays not as a ghost to be soothed but as a prosecutor with perfect memory. The house is not haunted by spirits; it is haunted by accuracy. Edie spent thirty years editing the night — Wren pushed her to safety, Wren was the brave one, Wren was lost saving her — and the house plays the night without the edits: the doorway, the choice, the lie a frightened child told a deputy to avoid blame, the search party that looked in the wrong field because of it. It optimizes for one intolerable thing: forcing the unedited reconstruction of one night into the daylight of Edie's adult mind — not revenge, not haunting, but the correction of the record. *You were not saved; you chose; and the one you owe can never be paid back, because she is the one you spent.* Edie's own professional creed — narrate every death but your own — is the doctrine the house turns against her until she finally performs it for her sister.

## Key Characters

**Wren Calloway** — the older sister, nine years dead at the story's start in the family's telling, eleven and alive in the replays. She is not a vengeful spirit but the night's most accurate witness: in the 1971 doorway she is the voice on the wrong side of the closing door, and in the culvert she is the death Edie was built to attend and never did. The series' rebirth runs through her — her remains recovered properly, her name cleared of the runaway story, her death finally witnessed instead of buried.

**The cousin** — a surviving relative who knew the family and still carries the rescue legend as gospel. Shown only a fragment of what Edie uncovers, the cousin concludes Wren ran off alone and thanks Edie for surviving so gracefully — the second jaw of the vise. To accept the sympathy is to leave Wren branded a runaway forever; the cousin is the person Edie must free with the truth, the proof that the debt is owed to the living as well as the dead.

## Series Engine

The engine is the house's room-by-room, decade-by-decade unedited reconstruction: each episode unlocks one more room and one more decade of the same winter night, peeling Edie's thirty-year edit back a single layer — and, critically, each episode runs its own discrete sub-investigation with an independent stake and a fair, partial false conclusion (one points the lie at the mother, one at the deputy, one at Edie's own buried memory), so the audience is actively re-solving rather than waiting for a signposted twist, while a present-tense pressure — the burning sale clock, the recovered-remains question, the town's settled legend — escalates outside the replays. The season is a controlled excavation toward a single doorway, and it ends closed. Structural runway exists only as a strict same-house anthology: a future season hands the house to a new owner decades on, inheriting Edie's now-true story and forced by the rooms to confront a debt of their own — the format intact (rooms as recursive witness, a self-told lie corrected by accurate space), the protagonist, decade, and crime fully replaced. The series is self-contained IP and expandable only as same-house, new-occupant witness anthology — never as serialized continuation that would cheapen a closed, devastating ending.
