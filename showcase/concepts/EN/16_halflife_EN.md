# Halflife

#### Logline
When a terminal drug-trial participant discovers the experimental compound saving her life also carries her dead mentor's awakened mind, she races the trial's final dose to keep him — until she learns the only way to stay herself is to let him die a second time.

#### Tagline
Every dose, one of them disappears.

## Revenue Thesis

*Halflife* is positioned at a premium animated-feature budget tier, where the production cost buys the one thing the concept cannot live without: two fully realized visual registers in a single body, rendered with the craft that makes the interior wound watchable rather than explained. The model is anchored to grounded genre features that converted a single high-concept hook into broad theatrical returns. *Unbreakable* (2000) turned a $75M budget into $248.1M worldwide — a 2.3x return — on the strength of a contained, character-first genre premise with a mid-film inversion, the same structural engine *Halflife* runs. *Far and Away* (1992) returned 1.3x on a $60M budget ($137.8M worldwide), a sober reminder that an emotionally sincere, star-driven drama can underperform when its hook is soft; *Halflife* protects against that floor by making its hook a hard, clocked thriller mechanism rather than a mood. At the high end, *Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom* (2018) returned 6.7x ($1.31B worldwide on $170M) — proof that a legible, four-quadrant genre concept can scale far past its budget when the campaign sells a single clear image. ## Why Now

Audiences have spent half a decade living inside the language of brain scans, gene therapy, and first-in-human trials — the vocabulary of saving a life has become household. At the same time, the culture has rediscovered animation as a medium for adult grief, not only children's adventure. *Halflife* sits exactly on that seam: a story that uses the literal physics of a dosing schedule to dramatize the most universal experience there is — wanting one more hour with someone who is already gone — at the precise moment audiences are ready to accept that an animated frame can hold an adult's heartbreak.

# 2. The Concept

## Mass-Appeal Theme

The human truth is the oldest one: a debt that cannot be repaid can only be inherited. You honor a life that saved yours not by giving it back — that is impossible — but by letting it finish inside you and change the shape of the person who keeps living. Every viewer who has sat beside a dying parent or grandparent knows the exact shape of the trap *Halflife* dramatizes — wanting to hold on, then slowly learning that holding on too hard is its own cruelty, and that letting go is the last gift love has left to give.

The convention it breaks is the expected animation-grief move. The genre's predictable next step is a whimsical magical-realism journey, or an "artificial mind learns to feel" arc. *Halflife* refuses both. The consciousness transfer is not a wish granted; it is the thing that must be deliberately, lovingly ended. And the mystery at the center is inverted: the protagonist hunts a saboteur for half the film, certain a rival lab is the enemy — and the saboteur turns out to be her own grief, working through her sleeping hands, trying to die *with* the person she lost rather than be saved by erasing him. The investigation does not catch a villain. It becomes a confession.

## Format & Genre

This is a story that cannot be told in live action without becoming a metaphor. The wound — *both versions of yourself are real* — is interior by nature, and live action can only gesture at it. Animation makes it literal and visible: two consciousnesses sharing one drawn body, each rendered in a distinct register, the balance between them clocked by the trial's dosing schedule. The animation is not decoration; it is the argument.

- **Genre:** Grounded emotional sci-fi drama / mystery-thriller (with a love-story spine)
- **Runtime:** 105–115 minutes
- **Budget Tier:** Premium animated feature

## Tonal Contract

Grounded emotional science fiction in the register of an adult-facing animated drama — intimate, scientifically literal, never campy. The animation carries the meaning: Mara's world is rendered in cool, exact, architectural line with saturated, precise pigment; Yusuf's surfacings arrive in warm, hand-painted, slightly trembling strokes that bleed into her frames and recede with each dose. Crucially, the color itself is the gauge. As the schedule strengthens Mara's pattern, her palette saturates and sharpens while his warmth desaturates toward grey; in the metabolic troughs where he wakes, his hand-painted warmth floods back and her precise line goes soft and bleeding. A viewer never needs the science explained — they can *see* who is winning the body by watching the pigment shift.

The lab is real and procedural; the stakes are medical and on a clock. The one fantastical conceit — an encoded mind awake inside a clinical trial — is treated with utter sincerity. The film is emotionally devastating but legible to a child who has lost a grandparent, and intellectually rigorous enough for an adult who works in a lab. The body-and-mind mechanics are kept legible but never graphic. No whimsy as wallpaper: every visual flourish carries grief.

# 3. Story

## Synopsis

Mara Okonkwo, 34, is a brilliant, emotionally armored neurochemist dying of a glioma that has resisted every standard therapy. The man who built her — Yusuf Adeyemi, who pulled her out of foster-system anonymity, taught her the brain is just memory wearing a body, and patiently taught a frightened child his left-handed way of drawing equations — dies of a stroke weeks before her last option opens: a first-in-human trial of a compound that rebuilds damaged neural tissue using a donor's mapped connectome as scaffolding. Yusuf, it turns out, signed his connectome to her. He gave her his mind to grow her brain back. The film's first ten minutes hold a single wordless image that teaches the audience everything: a warm, hand-painted brushstroke bleeding quietly into the cold architectural line of Mara's drawn world — grief made visible before a word of science is spoken.

The inciting incident lands not as a puzzle but as a gut-punch of intimacy. On dose three, Mara wakes at her bench having finished, in her own humming, a half-remembered lullaby — completing it with a private childhood mispronunciation only Yusuf ever knew, a tell no rival lab could fake and no memory could supply. He is not a memory she can mourn. He is awake, surfacing in the metabolic trough between doses, a second consciousness in warm trembling line against her cool precise one. And every dose the trial gives her strengthens her pattern and dissolves his. Twelve doses. By the twelfth, the body is hers alone. The clock is the protocol; the protocol cannot be reasoned with — skip a dose and the tumor returns, take them all and Yusuf is erased by the twelfth.

Mara, who would rather control a catastrophe than survive one, acts. She forges her own bloodwork and scans to hide that two distinct neural signatures are firing inside her, because an honest split result gets the trial pulled and kills her tumor's only treatment. She lies to the trial's lead investigator that the donor scaffold is integrating flawlessly, making herself the trial's unreliable narrator. She breaks into overnight storage and re-sequences her own dosing protocol to stretch the troughs and steal Yusuf more waking hours — knowingly trading her own recovery, and a flicker of returning tumor, for time with a dead man. And there is a face on the cold machine she is lying to: Dr. Elias Reyes, the principal investigator, who is shepherding this compound under the weight of his own buried stake in a clean endpoint, and whose belief in the protocol's integrity is the human wall Mara must keep deceiving. Then the trial begins to break from inside — corrupted compound batches, falsified integration logs. Mara investigates, certain a rival lab wants the intellectual property, tracking the saboteur through keycard logs, batch timestamps, a ghost user editing files at 3 a.m.

The midpoint reversal: the saboteur is her. The 3 a.m. user is Yusuf, working through her hands while she sleeps in the troughs, quietly poisoning the trial so it can never reach the twelfth dose — because he has done the math she refused to. The only ending where she lives is the ending where he is deliberately erased, and he will not let her become a woman who killed him to survive. He loves her too much to be saved by her loss of him. The investigation becomes a confession; she was never hunting an enemy, only looking at her own grief running in the dark. Now the debt is unbearable. She tries every repayment — overdosing to fuse both patterns (it would leave a non-functional hybrid, neither of them); surrendering control so she dissolves and he lives (the untreated tumor simply kills the body and both die). Each "repayment" is a way of not grieving, of keeping the ledger open so she never has to close him. Then, with three doses left, Reyes catches the forged data and halts the trial — and Mara is left with 72 hours of compound and a choice the protocol no longer makes for her.

In those final hours she stops trying to keep Yusuf as a living passenger or to repay an unpayable debt, and instead performs the one act of authorship the drug makes possible. She is not deleting him and she is not surrendering to him — she is translating him: turning an unbearable living presence into a carriable memory by deliberately encoding a chosen, finite set of his patterns into her own permanent connectome. Not Yusuf alive, not Yusuf erased — Yusuf metabolized. To make the inheritance real beyond her own skull, she does the thing Reyes' compromised endpoint could not survive: she authors an honest record of how the scaffold actually behaves — his left-handed math, his refusal to let people vanish — a true witness statement that future donor-recipient pairs will inherit, so the next person who carries a stranger is told the truth from the start. The twelfth-equivalent dose still comes. Yusuf the separate voice goes quiet. But Mara, drawing an equation in a hand that is now subtly, irreversibly both, is no longer the woman who memorized everyone to keep from losing anyone. She has lost him fully, kept him truly, and finally grieves.

## Emotional Arc

The film rises from clinical dread — a woman managing her own death like a spreadsheet — into the unbearable gift of a second chance with the person she lost, a warm and painful resurrection rendered in returning color. It climbs through the panic of a closing clock and the vertigo of discovering she is her own saboteur, peaks at the agony of an unpayable debt where every act of love is also an act of killing, and resolves not in triumph but in earned grief: the quiet, devastating relief of finally letting someone become finished. The audience feels the universal shape of loving a dying parent — wanting one more hour, then learning that holding on is its own cruelty, and that letting go is the last gift love can give.

# 4. Characters

## Protagonist

**Mara Okonkwo** — a neurochemist so terrified of losing people that she has made herself unforgettable to everyone and close to no one. She memorizes others perfectly while refusing to be fully present, so that grief can never surprise her again. Her **want** is to survive the trial *and* keep her dead mentor Yusuf alive inside her — to refuse the choice between her own life and his memory. Her **need** is to grieve: to accept that loving someone means letting them become finished, a person you carry rather than a person you keep running.

## Antagonist

**The Titration Schedule — and Yusuf's surviving will inside it.** The protocol is the cold antagonist: twelve fixed doses, each one strengthening Mara's neural pattern and dissolving Yusuf's. It cannot be bargained with. Skip a dose and the tumor returns; take every dose and Yusuf is gone by the twelfth. The schedule optimizes for one thing only — a clean clinical endpoint — indifferent to which mind is left standing, and it wears a human face in **Dr. Elias Reyes**, the principal investigator whose buried personal stake in a successful endpoint makes him the protocol's believer and the wall Mara must keep deceiving. The warm antagonist is Yusuf himself, who surfaces in the gaps between doses and secretly sabotages the trial to slow it down — because he would rather they both die together than be erased so she can live. He optimizes to never again be the cause of someone's loss: the same wound that made him save Mara in the first place.

## Key Characters

**Yusuf Adeyemi** — the mentor who pulled Mara out of foster-system anonymity and gave her both a mind and a way of seeing it. Dead before the film begins, awake inside it. He is the love-story spine and the moral engine: the man whose final act of love is to make himself the trial's saboteur so the woman he saved never has to survive by killing him.

**Dr. Elias Reyes** — principal investigator of the trial, the human face of the indifferent protocol. His own compromised stake in a clean result makes him simultaneously the obstacle Mara deceives and, by the end, the witness her honest record is written for.
