# Aftercare

#### Logline
When a flawless intensive care unit is blamed for a child's death, its most trusted nurse is appointed to lead the inquiry and clear the surgeon she loves — but each witness she interviews peels back a layer of the night, until the uncontrollable variable that killed everyone reveals itself as a thirty-second choice she made and erased, forcing her to decide whether loyalty means burying the truth or whether the truest thing she can give him is to stop being his alibi.

#### Tagline
Every alarm she silenced was telling the truth.

## Revenue Thesis

The economics favor the contained register. A single-location ICU drama concentrates spend where audiences feel it — writing, performance, and the suffocating precision of the unit itself — and away from the visual-effects budgets that sink the margins of spectacle-led titles. ## Why Now

Audiences have been trained by a decade of institutional drama to distrust the institution and root for the lone truth-teller — and that reflex is exactly what this story turns against them, at the precise cultural moment when the limited-series form has matured into the default home for a single, self-contained moral argument. Alarm fatigue and the quiet, tolerated failures of overstretched healthcare systems are a live anxiety in every territory that has lived through a strained hospital. The appetite is for a thriller whose villain is not a conspiracy but a competence we were all taught to admire.

# 2. The Concept

## Mass-Appeal Theme

The human truth underneath the mystery is the one almost nobody admits aloud: doing everything right is not the same as being innocent. The whole culture of the modern professional rests on the promise that perfect procedure is a moral shield — follow every protocol, pass every audit, and the guilt belongs to someone else. *Aftercare* takes the person who believes that most completely and makes her excellence the murder weapon. The genre convention it breaks is the outward arrow of the death-mystery: the detective is supposed to hunt the negligent party and expose the rotten system. Here the appointed investigator is the cause, the institution is guilty in a duller and truer way than any cabal — it tolerated a known problem for years — and the inquiry built to look outward eats its way back to the investigator's own hand. No conspiracy. No villain to unmask. Only the slow horror of realizing the person you were rooting for was always the answer.

## Format & Genre

The story is built for the eight-episode limited form because the inversion is a thing that has to happen in time, not in a single reveal. A feature could only stage the outward investigation and one twist; the series lets the lens physically rotate from forensic procedure to confession across episodic hours, so the audience lives the reversal as duration. Each episode is one peeled layer of the same fatal night, and the format is the argument.

- **Genre:** Drama-thriller / forensic mystery with a central romance
- **Runtime:** Eight episodes, roughly 50 minutes each
- **Budget Tier:** Contained mid-budget prestige limited series (single principal location)

## Tonal Contract

A forensic, clinically precise drama in the register of a procedural that slowly metabolizes into a confession — the patient obsession of a case that will not let go, grafted onto the intimacy of a working ICU. Cold blues and machine light against the human warmth of a love story under fluorescent strain. The thrill is investigative: each episode peels one more layer off the same night. But the engine underneath is moral and domestic — a woman, a grieving mother, and a man who trusts her too much. No gore-spectacle. No system cackling in the dark. The dread is procedural and intimate. The promise to the viewer: you come for the mystery of who killed the boy, and you stay because the answer implicates the one person you were rooting for.

# 3. Story

## Synopsis

Bed nine of a flagship surgical intensive care unit holds Tomas Rede, eight years old, post-operative and stable — until, on a quiet night shift, his oxygen quietly fails and he is gone before the alarms make sense. The unit's metrics are spotless. Its surgeon, Lior Asante, is a star. Its charge nurse, Mara Veld, ran the room and has never lost a child she was told she could save. When Tomas's mother accuses the hospital of killing her son, administration does the thing that looks like courage and is actually self-protection: it appoints Mara — the most trusted clinician on the floor, the woman who does everything right — to lead the internal root-cause review. Mara takes it to win it. She loves Lior; she will clear him, clear the unit, and close the family's grief under a clean, defensible chain of causation. The marriage they keep postponing is waiting on the other side of a sealed file.

She begins by looking outward, and for five hours the evidence rewards her. The ventilator's vendor turns out to have issued a genuine prior recall on an oxygen-sensor lot; for two episodes the machine itself is the most credible killer in the room, and the audience commits to it. Then the resident who first found the boy is caught having quietly back-dated a timestamp on his own chart — a falsified record that makes him look, for an episode, like a panicked man covering a fatal delay. Mara builds the case that the death was an uncontrollable variable: a rare equipment fault, a one-in-ten-thousand sensor lag, a frightened junior doctor. She does everything right. She *causes* the investigation — subpoenas the device vendor, re-runs the resuscitation in high-fidelity simulation, corners the resident, forces the raw pump and alarm logs out of an IT department that wants the maintenance ticket left sealed. Each move is meant to point the arrow away from the unit. Each move, by its own logic, swings it a single degree closer to her.

The series is told as the review's own document: each of the eight hours reconstructs the same fatal window from one new witness's point of view — the resident, the pharmacy technician, the night charge nurse, the grieving mother, the surgeon, and at last Mara herself. The thirty-second window at the center is replayed in nearly every episode, and the first two times the audience sees it, it reads as exculpatory — a routine line-check, a nurse doing her job, proof of innocence. Threaded through every hour is a single recurring image: the empty pediatric bed in bay nine being stripped and remade for the next child, the unit's machinery of recovery grinding forward over the body of the question. Then the midpoint turns the lens around. The device data exonerates the machine — the sensor never lagged, the recall lot was never installed, the line was fine. The resident's falsified timestamp was hiding an affair, not a death. Which means a human silenced an alarm, and the timestamp lands inside the thirty-second window Mara has no memory of: the window she logged as a routine line-check. Exhausted at hour fifty of a double shift, she had muted a "nuisance" alarm she was certain was a false positive — the unit's most chronic, most ignored failure — and walked four steps to help another coding patient. The unit's alarm system had been tuned to distrust exactly that kind of outlier instinct; the pattern she silenced had correlated with false positives in the overwhelming majority of cases, and she had been right about it ninety-nine times. The hundredth was Tomas. Her competence — the pattern-recognition that made her the best in the building — is precisely what made the fatal exception invisible to her.

Now the inquiry she built to look outward has turned the lens all the way around, and she alone holds the evidence. Lior, still believing in her completely, signs off on her draft findings — *equipment-attributable, no individual fault* — and proposes they finally marry once the unit reopens. Loyalty to him, to the unit, to her own intact life, requires her to file the clean lie he has now put his signature on. Truth requires her to hand the mother the name of the person who muted the alarm: herself. Both are owed to the same man — he deserves a future, *and* he deserves not to be made an accomplice to a forged review. Mara cannot pick a side, and the series refuses to let her. In the eighth hour she stops asking whether to protect him or expose herself and asks instead what the alarm itself was for. The muting was not a private failure — it was the unit's institutionalized habit, a chronic false-positive epidemic the staff silenced nightly because the hospital had never funded a fix. So she files neither the lie nor a private confession. She files the truth as a system: she names her own hand on the alarm *and* submits it as the proof case that the unit's alarm-fatigue protocol was engineered to produce exactly this death, in exactly this body, eventually. And the resolution costs her everything it cannot buy back. The hospital's counsel quietly buries the systemic indictment as inadmissible liability; what survives in the public record is the personal confession alone. Tomas's mother, handed a system instead of a name, rejects the framing to Mara's face — she did not want a protocol, she wanted someone to hate, and a woman who turns her dead son into a policy memo has stolen even that from her. Mara loses the marriage, the role, and likely her license. The fusion of loyalty and truth is real, and it is genuinely lossy: she is not allowed to keep her integrity and her righteousness at once. She becomes the cause that rewires every alarm in the building, and almost no one thanks her for it.

## Emotional Arc

The rise is seductive: a competent, beloved woman is trusted to set things right, and the audience wants her to win — every interview tightens the case, every exoneration of the machine feels like vindication, the love story with Lior glows under the worst light in the world. The turn curdles that trust into dread: the same competence that earned our faith is revealed as the weapon, and the detective is the killer. The fall is a descent through denial, the temptation to file the lie, the unbearable arrival of Lior's signature and his proposal exactly when she can least accept them. The floor is the moment she nearly buries it all and chooses love. The lift out of it is not triumph — it is the hard-won peace of someone who stops being innocent in order to finally be honest, and who learns the same night that being honest will not be rewarded, forgiven, or even understood. The felt shape is the universal terror of grief and culpability: that doing everything right is not the same as being guiltless, and that real love sometimes means refusing to be forgiven.

# 4. Characters

## Protagonist

**Mara Veld**, forty-one, charge nurse of a top surgical intensive care unit — the colleague every chart, every audit, and every grieving family is told to trust. Her inner contradiction is the engine of the whole series: she believes competence is a moral shield, that if she follows every protocol perfectly she has earned the right to be free of guilt. It is a belief she built not as arrogance but as armor — she is a bereavement-fluent professional who can sit with any family's loss and cannot, will not, grieve her own, because grieving would mean admitting that following protocol exactly does not keep the bad thing from being yours. That is precisely why she cannot allow herself to remember the one moment she didn't follow it. Her **want** is to prove the unit and the surgeon she loves did nothing wrong, close the inquiry clean, and keep the life she built intact. Her **need** is to accept that doing everything right does not make her innocent — that competence is not absolution, and that loving someone means refusing to let him be saved by her silence.

## Antagonist

**The Inquiry itself** — a hospital-mandated root-cause review that Mara is appointed to run — embodied through **Lior Asante**, the surgeon she loves, whose innocence depends on her not looking too closely. Its internal logic is what makes it merciless: a root-cause review exists to find the single failure point and follow the chain backward to its origin. Lior's entire defense is total trust in Mara's competence; he wants the review run by the one person who would never suspect herself, never imagining she is both judge and cause. The system's relentless backward logic and his love both point the same arrow at the same place — her. It optimizes for **closure**: the review wants a clean, attributable cause that lets the unit reopen and reassures families; Lior wants Mara's peace and their shared future. Both want the case sealed — which is exactly what makes the buried truth so dangerous, because sealing it cleanly requires a lie, and the only honest cause is the one neither of them can survive naming.

## Key Characters

**Tomas Rede's mother** is the moral floor of the series and the answer to its tidiest temptation. She arrives wanting a murderer and a name, and the synthesis Mara reaches — a death reframed as a fixable institutional design — is precisely the thing she refuses to accept. Her rejection in the final hour is the cost that the elegant resolution does not pay: a grieving woman is owed a person to hate, and a system is not a person. She keeps the story honest by denying it a clean win.

## Series Engine

The limited form is load-bearing, not decorative. Each of the eight episodes reconstructs the same fatal night from one new witness's point of view — resident, pharmacy technician, night charge nurse, the grieving mother, Lior, and finally Mara herself — so the lens physically rotates from outward investigation to inward confession in episodic real time, an inversion a feature could never earn. Per-episode hooks compound it: a new witness whose testimony reframes the prior episode, a recurring thirty-second window that means something different (and at first exculpatory) each time it replays, and the empty pediatric bed remade for the next child as the season's ticking spine. The story resolves fully as a true limited series, but it seeds a renewable anthology engine: each subsequent season takes a different perfect-record institution and a different trusted professional appointed to investigate it — a transplant board, a neonatal unit, an air-ambulance service — and forces that person to author their own indictment under the same vise, loyalty owed to one person colliding with a truth that can only be told by destroying them both. The patient-handover mechanic that makes each death deniable recurs as the connective tissue across seasons: the system's failures always travel in the gap where one professional hands a life to the next.
