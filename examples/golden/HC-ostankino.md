# The Tower

## Logline

Moscow, August 19, 1991. The Ostankino television tower is on fire during the
failed coup broadcast. Two married journalists — Dmitri, a committed Party
loyalist who has given his life to state television, and Irina, a closeted
dissident who has secretly been feeding material to foreign correspondents for
six years — are trapped in the tower elevator together for exactly ninety
minutes while the coup collapses outside and the fire closes in above them.
They cannot save their marriage without lying. They cannot tell the truth
without ending it.

## Untapped Asset

The 1991 Soviet coup attempt is one of the most-filmed political events of the
twentieth century, yet the Ostankino Tower — the broadcast nerve center whose
control was the coup's primary strategic objective — has never been dramatized
as an intimate space. The tower is historically documented: coup forces
surrounded it, crowds defended it, and on the evening of August 19 a fire broke
out in one of the technical floors.

The untapped asset is not the coup itself but the tower's interior. Ostankino
was the literal instrument of Soviet narrative control — every moment of official
reality for 280 million people was broadcast from this building. Trapping two
people who embody the Soviet information system's two poles (the true believer
and the covert dissident) inside the machine they have both served, as the
machine itself burns, is a setting that has existed for thirty-five years without
a film.

Source material: The coup's broadcast timeline is documented in primary Soviet
media archives. Eyewitness accounts from Ostankino staff confirm the fire and
evacuation chaos. The technical layout of the 1970s-era elevator system is
preserved in engineering records. All of this is verifiable history; none of it
has been used.

## Psychological Tension (SDT)

**Primary need: Relatedness**

Dmitri and Irina's marriage is real — eleven years, shared apartment, shared
professional identity, shared performance of the Soviet intellectual couple they
have learned to be. The relatedness deprivation is activated by the
simultaneous collapse of three things: the political system that gave their
relationship its context, the professional roles that gave them their shared
purpose, and the lie that has been the third person in their marriage for six
years. To belong to each other they must become strangers to who they have been.

**Secondary need: Autonomy**

The elevator is a physical literalization of the ideological trap they have each
been living. Irina has not been free — she has been performing freedom in secret,
which is not the same thing. Dmitri has not been constrained — he has chosen his
constraints so thoroughly that he cannot imagine a self that would choose
differently. The autonomy crisis is mutual: ninety minutes to discover whether
either of them has an autonomous self that can survive outside the roles that
defined them.

**Deprivation amplifier:** Their professional positions mean that their personal
truth directly affects the broadcast that could end or extend the coup. Irina
has a recording. Dmitri knows she has it. The personal and the historical are
not parallel — they are the same decision.

## TRIZ Contradiction

**The contradiction (State Broadcasting):** To broadcast the truth about the
coup's collapse (Irina's goal), she must use the state broadcasting apparatus
she has spent six years working against. The instrument of state narrative
control is the only instrument available for the counter-narrative. She cannot
tell the truth without using the system designed to prevent her from telling it.

**The contradiction (Marriage):** To preserve the marriage (Dmitri's implicit
goal throughout the film), he must suppress the truth she has just confessed.
But the moment she confessed, the truth became real — not suppressing it is no
longer possible, only pretending to suppress it. He cannot protect the marriage
without destroying what the marriage was protecting.

**Resolving principle:** TRIZ Principle 40 — "Composite Materials." The solution
exists in the zone between the contradictions: Dmitri does not broadcast Irina's
recording. He broadcasts silence — pulls the coup feed and transmits dead air
for forty-seven seconds, which is itself a signal to anyone monitoring who
understands what Ostankino silence means. The truth goes out without being
spoken. The marriage survives the night. The lie becomes impossible in the
morning.

## Polti / Tobias Mapping

**Polti Situation 18 — Crimes of Love**

The crime here is not romantic transgression but epistemic: Irina has loved
Dmitri across a lie so fundamental that the love itself is implicated. The
question the situation poses is not "can they forgive each other" but "can what
they had retroactively be called love if it was built on a structure she
controlled and he could not see." The crime is the asymmetry.

**Tobias Plot 7 — Forbidden Love**

The forbidden element is not romance but the identity each would be if free.
The plot structure follows the pattern: two people whose authentic selves are
incompatible with the social structure surrounding them find themselves in a
space where the social structure has temporarily collapsed — and must discover
whether the authentic selves are compatible with each other.

## The Irreversible Moment

**Minute 52:** The fire reaches the floor above them. The elevator shaft fills
with smoke. Irina understands that she may die in this elevator with her
husband still believing she is who he married. She tells him. Not as a
confession — as a correction. "I want you to know who actually worked beside
you." Dmitri's response — and the film's pivot — is not anger but something
worse: "I know. I always knew. I needed you not to tell me." The lie was shared.
The irreversibility is that both of them now know the other knows.

## Cinematic Parallels

**Parallel 1 — 12 Angry Men (1957):** A single enclosed space, a fixed time
window, and a political question that is also a personal one. The film's power
comes from the space's inability to release anyone until the question is
resolved. The Tower has the same structural advantage plus the external
historical crisis pressing against the walls.

**Parallel 2 — The Lives of Others (2006):** An East German surveillance
officer discovers that the people he is watching are more alive than he is. The
Tower inverts this: the person being watched (Irina) has been more alive than
her watcher (the Party apparatus Dmitri represents) all along, but has been
performing the watched role. The question is not whether surveillance corrupts
the watcher but whether the watched can survive the revelation that surveillance
was the only form of intimacy available.

**Parallel 3 — Before Midnight (2013):** Two intelligent people in a confined
space excavate a relationship until they find the thing they have been talking
around for years. The Tower compresses this into ninety minutes of crisis rather
than a European summer, but the excavation structure is the same.

## Production Notes

**Budget tier:** Chamber drama. Principal photography requires a single elevator
set built to 1970s Soviet engineering specifications (documented in historical
records) plus exterior shots of the actual Ostankino tower (available for
filming, landmark status). CGI required only for fire effects and exterior
crowd scenes. Mid-range budget achievable.

**Language:** Russian throughout. International release with subtitles. English-
language remake rights are a secondary revenue stream given the universal
premise.

**Key casting:** Both leads must be approximately 35–45 years old (established
journalists, eleven-year marriage plausible). Dmitri requires the specific type:
genuinely convinced, not cynical — the True Believer who does not perform belief
but inhabits it. Irina requires an actress capable of sustaining concealment and
revelation simultaneously.

**Historical rights:** All events depicted are documented public history. No
biographical rights required — characters are fictional composites of the
journalist class present at Ostankino on August 19, 1991.

**Distribution path:** Russian-language theatrical release (limited by current
sanctions context, but diaspora markets in Germany, Israel, United States, Canada
are accessible). Festival circuit entry via Berlin or Tribeca. Streaming
acquisition by platforms with post-Soviet catalog (Mubi, Criterion Channel).
