From history, political philosophy, systems theory, and civilizational analysis, list 15 documented types of civilizational-scale crises where what was threatened was not a single institution or nation but a foundational assumption shared across multiple civilizations. For each: (1) describe what is at stake in plain English — the assumption being threatened, (2) cite historical examples from multiple civilizations where this type of crisis occurred, (3) estimate the global audience in millions who would experience this threat personally, (4) identify which audience would have OPPOSITE responses to the same crisis (the divisiveness engine). Draw from: threats to epistemological foundations (how we know what is real), threats to social contract assumptions (why we cooperate), threats to temporal continuity (the future will resemble the past), threats to the value of human-generated work, threats to the reliability of institutional memory, threats to the distinction between natural and manufactured. Sources: Toynbee, Diamond, Harari, Taleb, Meadows, Tainter collapse theory, IPCC, WHO, academic civilizational studies.
